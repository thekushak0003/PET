Foundations

Structured Thinking

Consulting is about solving ambiguous problems with rigour and clarity. Structured thinking is the single most important skill evaluated in every consulting placement round.

The Consulting Mindset

Structure First

Never jump to solutions. Break the problem into parts before analysing any part. A structured wrong answer beats an unstructured right one.

Data-Driven

Form a hypothesis, then test it with data. Distinguish fact from assumption. Quantify whenever possible.

So What? Test

Every insight must lead to a recommendation. Ask "so what does this mean for the client?" after every finding.

The Pyramid Principle (Barbara Minto)

The most used communication framework in consulting. Structure communication top-down: Answer First → Supporting Arguments → Supporting Data.

Pyramid Structure
RECOMMENDATION / ANSWER
Key Argument 1
Key Argument 2
Key Argument 3
Data / Evidence
Data / Evidence
Analysis
Data / Evidence
Analysis
💡 SCR (Situation–Complication–Resolution): A variant of the Pyramid Principle for storytelling. Situation = what's happening now. Complication = why the current approach fails. Resolution = our recommended path forward.

How Consultants Approach Any Problem

1
Clarify the problem — What does success look like? What is in/out of scope? What constraints exist? Never start without understanding the real question.
2
Structure the analysis — Build a MECE issue tree. Identify the 2–3 most important branches to explore first.
3
Form a hypothesis — Based on initial information, hypothesise the most likely root cause or answer. Think like a doctor: "My initial hypothesis is X, and I'll test it."
4
Prioritise and analyse — Use the 80/20 rule (Pareto principle). Focus effort on the analyses most likely to prove or disprove your hypothesis.
5
Synthesise and recommend — Distil findings into actionable, prioritised recommendations. Answer: What? So what? Now what?

MECE Principle

MECE — Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive — is the bedrock of all consulting frameworks. It ensures analyses have no overlaps and no gaps.

What MECE Means

Mutually Exclusive (ME)

No overlap between categories. Each item belongs to exactly one bucket. Prevents double-counting and confusion.

Test: If something fits in two buckets, the buckets are not ME.

Collectively Exhaustive (CE)

All possibilities are covered. No item falls outside the categories. Prevents blind spots in analysis.

Test: Can you think of any case not covered by your list?

MECE Examples

Context Non-MECE (bad) MECE (good)
Segmenting customers "Young, old, and loyal customers" — overlapping categories By age: 0–18, 19–35, 36–55, 56+ — clean buckets
Revenue analysis "Online sales and international sales" — a customer can be both By channel: online vs offline (ME); by geography: domestic vs international (ME)
Cost breakdown "Fixed costs, labour, and overhead" — labour is a subcategory of fixed costs Fixed costs vs Variable costs (complete split at one level)
Declining profits "Revenue fell and costs rose" — can be both simultaneously Revenue issue tree (price × volume) || Cost issue tree (fixed || variable) — separate branches

Common MECE Splitting Approaches

By Formula

Profit = Revenue − Costs. Each driver is mutually exclusive. Classic for financial problems.

By Process / Journey

Awareness → Interest → Consideration → Purchase → Retention. Each stage is sequential and non-overlapping.

By Segment

B2B vs B2C, domestic vs international, product A vs B vs C. Each observation belongs to one group.

By Factor

Internal vs External, short-term vs long-term, controllable vs uncontrollable.

By Geography

North / South / East / West zones; country by country; city vs rural.

By Time

Before vs during vs after an event. Historical vs current vs future state.

⚠️ MECE pitfalls: Being MECE at the top level but not at sub-levels. Creating so many buckets that structure becomes unwieldy. Spending too long ensuring perfect MECE instead of making progress. Aim for "good enough MECE" in a time-pressured case.

Issue Trees & Problem Decomposition

An issue tree is a visual, MECE breakdown of a problem into its component parts. It is the primary tool for structuring any business problem.

Logic Tree vs Issue Tree vs Hypothesis Tree

Type Purpose Direction Use When
Logic Tree (Why?) Decompose causes of a problem Drill down into causes Root cause analysis: "Why are profits falling?"
Issue Tree (What?) Identify all issues to investigate Neutral decomposition of problem space Unknown problems: "What could be causing X?"
Hypothesis Tree Test a specific hypothesis Start with answer, find evidence You have a strong initial view to prove/disprove

Example: "Why are our profits declining?"

Profits Declining
Revenue ↓
Costs ↑
Revenue branch:
Price ↓
Volume ↓
Market price ↓
Pricing strategy change
New customers ↓
Retention ↓
Cost branch:
Fixed Costs ↑
Variable Costs ↑
Rent / capex
Headcount
Raw materials ↑
Supply chain inefficiency
💡 Breadth before depth: Build out all branches to the same level before diving deep into any one. This ensures you don't miss important issues by over-indexing on the first thing you see.

Issue Tree Best Practices

  • Use MECE splits at each level — every branch should be non-overlapping and exhaustive
  • 3–5 branches per level — fewer looks incomplete, more becomes unwieldy
  • Use nouns for issues, verbs for hypotheses — "Revenue" vs "Revenue has fallen due to lost customers"
  • Prioritise after building — once the tree is built, mark which 2–3 branches are most critical to investigate
  • Keep it visual — always draw it, even in verbal cases ("imagine I'm drawing a tree…")

Hypothesis-Led Thinking

Top consulting firms expect candidates to form a hypothesis early and drive the analysis to test it, rather than collecting all data first.

Why Hypothesis-Led?

In real consulting, clients pay by the hour. Boiling the ocean (analysing everything) is expensive and slow. A hypothesis directs effort to the most impactful analyses first.

🎯 The Consulting Detective analogy: Sherlock Holmes doesn't wait to collect all evidence before forming a theory. He forms a hypothesis immediately from available clues, then tests and refines it. Consultants do the same.

Good vs Bad Hypotheses

Criterion Bad Hypothesis Good Hypothesis
Specificity "Something is wrong with revenue" "Revenue is declining because new customer acquisition fell by 30% in the last two quarters"
Testability "The company needs to innovate more" "Introducing a subscription model will increase LTV by 25% within 12 months"
Directional "Costs might be too high" "Variable costs have increased due to a spike in raw material prices, not operational inefficiency"

The 80/20 Rule (Pareto Principle) in Consulting

80% of the impact comes from 20% of the causes. In consulting this means:

  • Focus on the 2–3 most critical issues, not all 10 possible issues
  • Prioritise analyses with the highest expected value (impact × probability)
  • A good enough answer fast is more valuable than a perfect answer slow

SWOT Analysis

SWOT maps a company's internal capabilities against external market realities. It is the most widely known strategy framework — but must be used thoughtfully, not as a superficial list.

The Four Quadrants

Strengths (Internal, Positive)

  • What does the company do better than competitors?
  • Unique resources, capabilities, or assets
  • Strong brand, patents, loyal customer base
  • Cost advantages, superior technology

Weaknesses (Internal, Negative)

  • What areas need improvement?
  • Resource gaps, skill shortages
  • Poor brand perception in certain segments
  • High cost structure, outdated technology

Opportunities (External, Positive)

  • Emerging market trends to exploit
  • Regulatory changes opening new markets
  • Competitors' weaknesses to capitalise on
  • Underserved customer segments

Threats (External, Negative)

  • New entrants or substitute products
  • Shifting consumer preferences
  • Regulatory or geopolitical risks
  • Supply chain vulnerabilities

TOWS Matrix — Turning SWOT Into Strategy

SWOT lists observations. TOWS converts them into strategic actions by combining quadrants:

Opportunities Threats
Strengths SO — Maxi-Maxi
Use strengths to seize opportunities. Aggressive growth strategies.		 ST — Maxi-Mini
Use strengths to counter threats. Defensive strategies using core advantages.
Weaknesses WO — Mini-Maxi
Overcome weaknesses to exploit opportunities. Invest in development.		 WT — Mini-Mini
Minimise weaknesses and threats. Defensive, consolidation strategies.
💡 Interview tip: Never stop at listing SWOT items. Always move to implications: "Given this strength and that opportunity, the company should…" The TOWS extension shows strategic depth.

Porter's Five Forces

Developed by Michael Porter at Harvard, this framework analyses the competitive intensity and attractiveness of an industry. It determines long-run industry profitability.

The Five Forces

⬆ Threat of New Entrants
How easy is it for new competitors to enter? Barriers: economies of scale, capital requirements, brand loyalty, regulatory hurdles, proprietary tech.
◀ Bargaining Power of Suppliers
Can suppliers raise prices or reduce quality? High when few suppliers exist, inputs are unique, or switching costs are high.
INDUSTRY RIVALRY
Number of competitors, growth rate, product differentiation, switching costs, exit barriers
Bargaining Power of Buyers ▶
Can buyers push prices down? High when buyers are large, products are undifferentiated, or many substitutes exist.
⬇ Threat of Substitutes
Can buyers switch to a different product/service fulfilling the same need? High when substitutes are cheaper, better quality, or switching costs are low.

Interpreting Porter's Five Forces

Force High force → industry is… Low force → industry is… Key Questions
Threat of New Entrants Less attractive (profits erode) More attractive (incumbents protected) What barriers to entry exist? How high is capital requirement?
Supplier Power Costly for firms (squeezed margins) Firms can negotiate better terms How many suppliers? Can we backward-integrate?
Buyer Power Pressure on prices (margin squeeze) Firms can charge premium prices How concentrated are buyers? What are switching costs?
Threat of Substitutes Caps industry pricing power Industry can charge premium What's the price-performance of substitutes?
Industry Rivalry Price wars, margin erosion Stable, profitable competition How many firms? Is the market growing?
⚠️ Critique of Porter's 5 Forces: Static snapshot — doesn't capture dynamic change. Ignores complementors (e.g., app developers for a platform). Assumes zero-sum competition; misses co-opetition. Best used alongside PESTLE and internal capability analysis.

Quadrant Cash Flow Strategy Real Example

McKinsey 7-S Framework

The 7-S model analyses organisational effectiveness by examining seven interdependent internal elements. All seven must be aligned for the organisation to function well.

The Seven Elements

Hard Elements (easier to change)

Element Description
Strategy The plan to build competitive advantage. How the firm wins in its markets.
Structure How the organisation is divided. Hierarchy, reporting lines, divisions.
Systems Daily processes and procedures. IT systems, HR processes, reporting routines.

Soft Elements (harder to change)

Element Description
Shared Values Core values and culture — the centre of the model. Influences all other elements.
Style Leadership style and management behaviour. Culture at the top.
Staff The people — number, type, and general capabilities of workforce.
Skills Actual skills and competencies of employees. Core competencies of the firm.

When to Use the 7-S Framework

  • Post-merger integration — aligning two organisations' structures, systems, and cultures
  • Organisational transformation or change management
  • Diagnosing why a strategy is not being executed effectively
  • Identifying gaps between current and desired state across all 7 elements
💡 The key insight of 7-S: Strategy alone is insufficient. A brilliant strategy will fail if the structure, systems, people, and culture are misaligned with it. The "Soft Ss" (culture, skills, values) are often harder to change than the "Hard Ss" but matter just as much.

Marketing

4 Ps / 7 Ps — The Marketing Mix

The Marketing Mix defines how a company brings a product or service to market. The original 4 Ps were extended to 7 Ps for services companies.

The 4 Ps (Products)

1. Product

What are you selling? Physical product, service, or experience? Key decisions: features, design, quality, branding, packaging, product range, lifecycle.

Ask: What problem does it solve? What is its USP? Which stage of PLC is it in?

2. Price

What will customers pay? Strategies: cost-plus, value-based, competitive, penetration, skimming, freemium, dynamic pricing.

Ask: What is the customer's WTP? How price-elastic is demand? What signals does price send about quality?

3. Place (Distribution)

How does it reach the customer? Channels: direct (website, store), indirect (distributors, retailers), omnichannel strategy. Coverage, logistics, convenience.

Ask: Where does the target customer shop? What are channel economics?

4. Promotion

How will you communicate and attract customers? Mix: advertising, PR, sales promotion, direct marketing, digital/social media, content marketing, SEO.

Ask: What is the customer journey? Where are the touchpoints? What message resonates?

Extended 7 Ps (Services)

Services differ from products because they are intangible, heterogeneous, and perishable. Three extra Ps address service-specific challenges:

5. People

All human actors in the service delivery — employees, customers, other customers. Especially critical in hospitality, healthcare, consulting. Training, culture, and appearance all matter.

6. Process

The procedures, mechanisms, and flow of activities used to deliver the service. Service blueprinting, efficiency, standardisation vs customisation, digital process vs human touch.

7. Physical Evidence

Tangible cues that signal service quality — the environment, décor, uniforms, receipts, website design. Since services are intangible, physical evidence reduces perceived risk.

💡 Interview application: When asked to launch a product or grow a brand, systematically run through all 4 (or 7) Ps. Don't skip Place — distribution strategy is commonly overlooked but critically important.
Marketing

STP — Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning

STP is the strategic process before the 4 Ps. You cannot build an effective marketing mix without first knowing who you're targeting and how you want to be perceived.

The Three Steps

S
Segmentation: Divide the total market into distinct groups of buyers with different needs, characteristics, or behaviours who might require separate products or marketing mixes.
T
Targeting: Evaluate each segment's attractiveness and select which segment(s) to serve. Decide on coverage strategy: undifferentiated, differentiated, concentrated, or micromarketing.
P
Positioning: Define how you want the target segment to perceive your offering relative to competitors. Create a positioning statement and design the marketing mix to deliver it.

Segmentation Variables

Type Variables Example
Geographic Country, region, city size, climate, urban/rural McDonalds adapts menu by country
Demographic Age, gender, income, education, occupation, family size Luxury cars targeting high-income 35–55 year olds
Psychographic Lifestyle, personality, values, social class, attitudes Patagonia targets environmentally-conscious consumers
Behavioural Usage rate, loyalty, benefits sought, purchase occasion Airlines segmenting frequent vs occasional flyers
Firmographic (B2B) Industry, company size, revenue, number of employees Salesforce targeting mid-market firms >100 employees

Criteria for Effective Segmentation (MADS)

  • Measurable — can you quantify the segment's size and purchasing power?
  • Accessible — can you effectively reach and serve this segment?
  • Differentiable — do segments respond differently to marketing stimuli?
  • Substantial — is the segment large or profitable enough to pursue?
  • Actionable — can you design effective programmes to attract and serve them?

Positioning Statement Template

📝 For [target segment] who [statement of the need or opportunity], [brand/product name] is a [product category] that [key benefit — compelling reason to buy]. Unlike [primary competitor], our product [statement of key differentiation].

Product Life Cycle (PLC)

Every product moves through four stages — Introduction, Growth, Maturity, Decline. Strategy must adapt at each stage.

The Four Stages

Stage Sales Profits Competition Strategic Focus Tactics
Introduction Low, growing slowly Negative (heavy investment) None or very few Awareness & trial Heavy promotion, selective distribution, skimming or penetration pricing
Growth Rapidly rising Rising, peak near end Increasing entrants Gain market share fast Expand distribution, improve product, moderate pricing
Maturity Peak, slowing Declining slowly Many, intense rivalry Defend share, extend life Competitive pricing, product modifications, new segments, promo efficiency
Decline Falling Low or negative Consolidating Harvest or divest Cut costs, selective distribution, reduce marketing spend, exit decision
💡 Extending the Maturity stage: Market modification (new users, more usage), product modification (new features, quality improvements), marketing mix modification (new channels, promotions). iPhone is a masterclass in extending product life through yearly iterations.

Profitability Framework

The most common case interview framework. "Profits are declining — why?" is asked at nearly every consulting first round. Nail this framework cold.

The Core Structure

Profitability Framework
Profit = Revenue − Costs
Revenue = Price × Volume
Price:
Pricing strategy · Discounting · Competitors · Mix shift (premium vs economy units)
Volume:
Number of customers × purchase frequency × units per transaction
New acquisition · Retention/churn · Market size · Market share
Costs = Fixed + Variable
Fixed Costs:
Rent · Depreciation · Salaries · Overheads · Technology infrastructure
Variable Costs:
Raw materials · Labour per unit · Packaging · Shipping · Sales commissions

Profitability Analysis — Key Questions to Ask

Area Questions to Ask the Interviewer
Scope & Context Is this decline company-wide or specific to a product/region/segment? When did it start? Is the industry also declining or only us?
Revenue Are prices the same? Has volume changed? What's the product mix shift? Are we losing customers or are they buying less?
Costs Have costs increased? Which costs — fixed or variable? Is it one-off (e.g., legal settlement) or ongoing? Relative to revenue or absolute?
Benchmarking How are competitors performing? Are they profitable? What does industry margin look like?
💡 Always benchmark: A 5% profit margin might be great in grocery retail but terrible in software. Compare against the company's historical trend AND against industry peers. This quickly narrows whether the problem is company-specific or industry-wide.

Market Entry Framework

"Should our client enter this new market?" — a classic case type at every major firm. Use this structured approach.

The Three Big Questions

1. Is the market attractive?

Market size, growth rate, profitability, competitive intensity (Porter's 5), regulation, customer needs.

2. Can we compete?

Do we have the capabilities, resources, relationships, and brand to win? What's our competitive advantage vs incumbents?

3. Is it worth it?

Expected ROI, required investment, timeline to profitability, risk-adjusted return, opportunity cost vs other investments.

Market Attractiveness Assessment

Dimension Key Questions
Market Size & Growth TAM/SAM/SOM? What is the growth rate? Secular trend or cyclical? Is it early stage or mature?
Profitability What are typical margins in this market? Who currently makes money and why?
Competition How many players? How entrenched? Any dominant player with network effects or high switching costs?
Customer Needs Are customers underserved? What's their willingness to pay? Are there underserved segments?
Regulatory / Macro Any legal barriers? Licences required? Geopolitical risks? ESG considerations?

Entry Mode Choices

Organic / Greenfield High Full High No suitable acquisition target; want custom build; time is not critical
Acquisition Very High Full Very High Need speed; target has capabilities/customers you lack; high synergies

M&A Framework

Mergers and Acquisitions cases appear in banking, strategy, and consulting interviews. Evaluate whether an acquisition creates value for the acquirer.

The Core M&A Question

🎯 Should the acquirer buy the target?
Value created = Synergies − Premium paid. A deal only makes sense if the synergies (over time) exceed the acquisition premium paid above market value.

M&A Evaluation Framework

1
Strategic Rationale — Why is the acquirer buying this target? Horizontal consolidation, vertical integration, new capability, new geography, eliminating a competitor, acqui-hire?
2
Target Assessment — Is the target a good business? Revenue growth, profitability, market position, management quality, customer base, technology/IP.
3
Synergy Analysis — Revenue synergies (cross-sell, new markets) and cost synergies (headcount, procurement, shared infrastructure). Quantify and timeline each.
4
Risks & Red Flags — Cultural mismatch, regulatory approval risk, customer/talent attrition, hidden liabilities, integration complexity, leverage risk.
5
Valuation & Affordability — Is the price fair? EV/EBITDA multiple vs peers. Can the acquirer afford it without jeopardising balance sheet health?
6
Integration Plan — What is the Day 1 plan? Which systems to integrate first? What's the timeline to realise synergies? Who leads integration?

Types of Synergies

Revenue Synergies

Cross-selling products to each other's customer base. Entering new geographies using target's distribution. Combined brand reputation commanding premium pricing. Usually harder to achieve and slower to materialise.

Cost Synergies

Eliminating duplicate functions (HR, Finance, IT). Combined purchasing power reducing procurement costs. Closing redundant facilities. Generally more certain and faster than revenue synergies.

Pricing Strategies

Pricing is one of the highest-leverage levers in any business — a 1% price increase typically creates a 10%+ improvement in operating profit. Know all major pricing strategies.

Core Pricing Approaches

Strategy How It Works Use When Risk
Cost-Plus Price = Cost × (1 + margin%). Simple, ensures profitability. Manufacturing, construction, government contracts Ignores customer WTP and competition. Can overprice or underprice.
Value-Based Price based on perceived value to customer and WTP, not cost. Most profitable approach. Differentiated products, B2B software, luxury Requires deep customer insight. Hard to determine WTP precisely.
Competitive Price at, above, or below competitors. Use competitor prices as anchor. Commoditised markets, price-sensitive segments Can lead to price wars. Profit only if costs are lower than competitors.
Penetration Launch low to gain share quickly, raise price later. Network-effect products, fast-growing markets Sets low-price expectation. Customers resist later increases.
Price Skimming Launch high to capture maximum WTP from early adopters, reduce over time. Tech products, innovative goods with few substitutes Invites competition quickly. Loyal early adopters feel exploited.
Dynamic Pricing Real-time price adjustments based on demand, time, inventory, user data. Airlines, hotels, ride-hailing, e-commerce Customer backlash (price discrimination). Regulatory scrutiny.
Freemium Basic version free; premium features paid. Convert free users to paying. SaaS, apps, digital products Low conversion rates (typically 2–5%). Free users are expensive to serve.
Subscription Recurring charge for continuous access. Predictable revenue. Software, media, services with ongoing use Churn management. Customers compare total cost over time.
Price Bundling Combine products/services at one price. Captures more value. Software suites, cable, fast food meals Can obscure individual product value. Unwanted items frustrate customers.
💡 The pricing interview framework: (1) What is the customer's WTP? (2) What are our costs? (3) What are competitors charging? (4) What pricing strategy aligns with our brand positioning? Then triangulate to a price range and recommend.

PESTLE Analysis

PESTLE examines the macro-environmental forces that affect a business or industry. Use it to identify external opportunities and threats (the "E" and "T" of SWOT).

The Six Dimensions

Factor What to Analyse Example Questions
P — Political Government policies, political stability, trade policy, tariffs, geopolitical risk Could a change in government policy restrict our operations? Are there sanctions on key markets?
E — Economic GDP growth, inflation, interest rates, unemployment, exchange rates, consumer spending How does a recession affect our demand? Are our costs sensitive to exchange rate movements?
S — Social Demographics, cultural trends, health consciousness, attitudes, lifestyle shifts Is an ageing population a growth opportunity? How is WFH culture affecting our market?
T — Technological R&D trends, automation, digitalisation, AI, cybersecurity, infrastructure Could AI automate our core service? Are we exposed to cybersecurity risks?
L — Legal Employment law, health & safety, consumer protection, IP, data privacy (GDPR) Does GDPR compliance add significant cost? Are our contracts protected by IP law?
E — Environmental Climate change, sustainability regulations, carbon pricing, ESG expectations Are we exposed to carbon taxes? What ESG standards do our key investors expect?
💡 PESTLE vs Porter's 5 Forces: PESTLE = macro-environment (outside the industry). Porter = competitive forces within the industry. Use PESTLE first to understand the context, then Porter's to analyse the competitive dynamics within that context.
Case Interview

Case Interview Structure

A case interview tests your ability to structure ambiguous business problems and communicate clearly under pressure. Mastering the structure is non-negotiable.

The Case Interview Format (30–45 min)

1
Understand the question (2–3 min) — Listen carefully. Take notes. Ask 1–2 clarifying questions to confirm scope, define success, and understand constraints. Never rush past this step.
2
Structure your approach (2–3 min) — Say "I'd like to take a moment to structure my thinking." Build a MECE issue tree. Tell the interviewer what you'll explore and why.
3
Analyse key branches (~20 min) — Proceed branch by branch. Ask for data. Do mental maths. Form and test hypotheses. Prioritise the 2–3 most important areas, not all of them.
4
Synthesise a recommendation (2–3 min) — Provide a clear, direct answer. "I recommend X because of reasons A, B, and C. The key risk is Y, which we can mitigate by Z." Never be wishy-washy.

Do's and Don'ts

DO's

  • Think aloud — interviewers want to see your process
  • Signpost transitions: "Moving on to costs..."
  • State hypotheses before asking for data
  • Sanity-check your numbers (is ₹1T revenue plausible?)
  • Ask for 1–2 minutes to structure when needed
  • Adapt structure when you receive new information
  • Be direct with your recommendation at the end

DON'Ts

  • Jump into an answer without structuring first
  • Go silent for more than 15 seconds
  • Present every possible issue equally — prioritise
  • Panic when the interviewer pushes back on your answer
  • Give a wishy-washy or multiple-choice recommendation
  • Forget to check the numbers make sense
  • Ignore clues the interviewer gives you about direction

The Recommendation Template

"Based on our analysis, I recommend that [client] should [specific action]."

"The primary reason is [Reason 1], which is supported by [evidence / data]. Additionally, [Reason 2]. The most significant risk is [Risk], but this can be mitigated by [mitigation]."

"The next steps would be: (1) [immediate action], (2) [short-term action], (3) [long-term action]."

Market Sizing (Fermi Estimation)

Market sizing tests your ability to estimate quickly with structured logic, not memorised numbers.

Two Approaches

Top-Down

Start with total population → narrow down by relevant filters

Example: "Market for yoga mats in India"
India population 1.4B → Adults (75%) = 1.05B → Urban (35%) = 368M → Exercise-conscious (20%) = 74M → Yoga practitioners (15%) = 11M → Buy a new mat every 3 years → ~3.7M mats/year × ₹1,500 avg = ₹5.5B market

Bottom-Up

Start with supply-side units × usage

Example: "Revenue of a busy coffee shop"
Seats: 40 × Turnover: 3x/hour × Operating hours: 8 hrs = 960 visits/day × ₹180 avg spend = ₹172,800/day × 300 operating days = ₹52M/year

💡 Market sizing tips: Round numbers for easy mental maths. State your assumptions explicitly before calculating. Sense-check your final answer: "Does ₹5.5B for yoga mats sound reasonable?" Ask if interviewer wants you to adjust any assumptions.
Case Interview

Top Interview Questions & Answers

The most commonly asked conceptual and framework questions across McKinsey, BCG, Bain, Deloitte, and similar firms.

Q1: What is the difference between SWOT and PESTLE?

PESTLE analyses macro-environmental factors entirely outside the company's control (political, economic, social, technology, legal, environmental). SWOT analyses both internal factors (Strengths, Weaknesses — the company's own capabilities) and external factors (Opportunities, Threats — often drawn from PESTLE). In practice, PESTLE is often used as input to populate the O and T of a SWOT analysis.

Q2: A company's revenue is flat but profits are falling — where do you start?

Since revenue is flat, the problem must be on the cost side. I'd investigate: (1) Have fixed costs increased — new hires, new leases, depreciation? (2) Have variable costs increased — raw material prices, logistics costs, higher cost-per-unit? (3) Has the product mix shifted toward lower-margin items (revenue flat but mix changed)? (4) Are there one-off costs (legal, restructuring)? I'd ask for a breakdown of costs by category and compare to the prior period and to industry benchmarks.

Q3: How do you assess whether a market is attractive?

I'd use a three-lens approach: (1) Market economics — size (TAM), growth rate, profitability of incumbents, cyclicality. (2) Competitive dynamics — Porter's Five Forces: barriers to entry, supplier/buyer power, substitutes, rivalry. (3) Strategic fit — Does our client have a right to win? Existing capabilities, relationships, brand that can be leveraged? A large, growing market is only attractive if we can compete effectively in it.

Q4: Why might a profitable company have a cash flow problem?

Common causes: (1) Working capital issues — collecting receivables slowly (long credit terms to customers) while paying suppliers quickly, or building up large inventory. (2) Heavy capex — investing in machinery/property that doesn't show immediately in P&L (depreciated, not expensed). (3) Seasonal business — profits accrue over time but cash is spent upfront on inventory or seasonal staff. (4) Rapid growth — fast growth requires upfront cash for stock, staff, and assets before revenue catches up. Key lesson: profit ≠ cash flow.

Q5: How would you price a new product no one has seen before?

Three-step triangulation: (1) Willingness to pay (WTP) — conduct customer research, use conjoint analysis, look at how much they currently spend on alternatives solving the same need. (2) Cost floor — ensure price covers variable costs at minimum; above break-even for profitability. (3) Competitive anchoring — what do substitutes cost? Even for a truly novel product, there are adjacent reference points. Then choose a pricing strategy: skim (high launch price, capture early adopters, lower later) or penetrate (low price, gain share fast, raise later). For B2B, always use value-based pricing — quantify the economic value created for the customer and price as a fraction of that.

Q6: When would you NOT recommend using Porter's Five Forces?

Porter's Five Forces has several limitations: (1) It's a static snapshot — doesn't capture how quickly digital industries evolve (e.g., a smartphone app could disrupt an industry overnight). (2) It ignores complementors — firms that enhance your product's value (e.g., app developers for Apple's ecosystem are critical to Apple but not captured as one of the 5 forces). (3) It assumes zero-sum competition and misses co-opetition (e.g., Samsung is both a supplier to and competitor of Apple). (4) Less useful for platform businesses or multi-sided markets where traditional industry boundaries are blurry. In these cases, supplement with a platform/network-effect analysis or a jobs-to-be-done framework.

Quick-Fire Concept Reference

Question Answer
What does MECE stand for? Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive — no overlaps, no gaps
What is the Pyramid Principle? Answer first, then support: lead with recommendation, follow with arguments and data
What is a "Cash Cow" in BCG? High market share, low growth market — generates cash, needs minimal reinvestment
4 Ps of marketing? Product, Price, Place (distribution), Promotion
Difference between penetration and skimming pricing? Penetration = low launch price to grab share; Skimming = high launch price to capture WTP of early adopters
What does Porter's Value Chain separate? Primary activities (directly create value) from Support activities (enable primary activities)
What does STP stand for? Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning
TOWS vs SWOT? SWOT lists findings; TOWS converts them into strategies by combining quadrants (SO, ST, WO, WT)
What are the two synergy types in M&A? Revenue synergies (cross-sell, new markets) and Cost synergies (headcount, procurement, shared assets)
Profit ≠ Cash Flow — why? Working capital changes, capex investment, seasonal timing, and rapid growth can all cause a profitable business to consume cash
