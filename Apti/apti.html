Aptitude tests assess reasoning, problem-solving, and comprehension ability. They appear in virtually every placement test across IT, consulting, banking, FMCG, and PSU sectors.

Exam Strategy & Time Management

The 3-Pass Strategy

1
Pass 1 — Easy wins (first 60% of time): Go through all questions. Answer everything you can solve in under 45 seconds. Skip and mark anything that takes longer. Build confidence and secure easy marks first.
2
Pass 2 — Medium questions (next 30% of time): Return to skipped questions that seem solvable. Use elimination. Apply shortcuts and tricks you've practised. Don't spend more than 90 seconds on any single question.
3
Pass 3 — Guess strategically (last 10%): With 1–2 minutes left, fill remaining blanks. Use elimination to narrow options even without solving fully. Never leave blank if no negative marking.

Section-wise Time Allocation (60-min test)

Section Typical Questions Allocated Time Time per Question
Verbal Ability 15–20 18–20 min ~60–70 sec
Non-Verbal / Logical 10–15 15–18 min ~70–90 sec
Quantitative Aptitude 15–20 20–25 min ~75–90 sec
Buffer / Review — 5 min —

Quick Wins — High ROI Strategies

Verbal Quick Wins

RC: Read the questions before the passage. Vocabulary: learn word roots (50 roots = 500+ words). Grammar: focus on subject-verb agreement and tense errors — appear in 60% of error-spotting.

Non-Verbal Quick Wins

Series: look for +n, ×n, and alternating patterns first. Mirror images: shapes do NOT flip, only direction reverses. Odd-one-out: usually one property (sides, symmetry, shading) that differs.

Quant Quick Wins

Learn divisibility rules for 2–13. Memorise squares up to 25 and cubes up to 15. Percentages: convert to fractions (25% = ¼) for speed. Use approximation — exact answers rarely needed.

Elimination Strategy

Even without solving: eliminate obviously wrong options (too large/small, wrong unit, opposite meaning). Reduces 4-choice to 2-choice → guess accuracy jumps from 25% to 50%.

■ Verbal

Reading Comprehension (RC)

RC passages test your ability to understand, infer, and analyse written text. Typically 2–4 passages with 4–7 questions each.

Question Types in RC

Question Type What It Tests Strategy
Main Idea / Central Theme Overall purpose of the passage Read first and last paragraphs. The main idea is usually stated or implied there.
Factual / Detail Specific information stated in text Scan for keywords from the question. Answer must be directly stated — not inferred.
Inference What can be concluded beyond what's stated The answer must follow logically from the passage. Watch for "too strong" options (always/never).
Tone / Attitude Author's emotional stance Look for adjectives and adverbs. Options: critical, appreciative, neutral, sarcastic, objective.
Vocabulary in Context Meaning of a word as used in the passage Re-read the surrounding sentence. The word may have an unusual usage — context is key.
Title Best heading for the passage Must cover all main points without being too specific or too broad.
Assumption Unstated premise the author relies on An assumption is something that MUST be true for the argument to hold.

The SQR3 Method for RC

S
Survey: Skim the passage in 30 seconds — read first sentence of each paragraph to get the structure.
Q
Questions first: Read all questions before reading the passage in full. Know what to look for.
R1
Read actively: Read the passage with the questions in mind. Underline key facts, names, dates, opinions.
R2
Recall: After reading, briefly summarise each paragraph in your head in one sentence.
R3
Review: Answer questions. For detail questions, go back to find the exact location. Don't rely on memory.

Sample RC Question

Passage (excerpt): "The Industrial Revolution, which began in Britain in the late 18th century, fundamentally transformed human society. It shifted economies from agrarian and handicraft bases to one dominated by industry and machine manufacturing. Critics argue that while it generated unprecedented wealth, it also created profound social inequalities, with factory workers enduring gruelling conditions for meagre wages."

Q: The author's tone toward the Industrial Revolution is best described as:
AWholly celebratory
BBalanced, acknowledging both benefits and drawbacks
CStrongly critical of industrialisation
DIndifferent and purely factual
✅ B — The passage uses "unprecedented wealth" (positive) and "gruelling conditions, meagre wages" (negative) — a balanced, nuanced view.
⚠️ Trap alert: "Too extreme" options (always, never, completely, entirely) are almost always wrong in RC. Real-world passages deal in nuance, not absolutes.

Verbal Analogies

Analogies test your ability to see relationships between word pairs. The key is identifying the EXACT type of relationship — not just a "similar" one.

Types of Analogy Relationships

Relationship Type Example Pattern
Part → Whole Chapter : Book First is a part of Second
Tool → Purpose Scalpel : Surgery First is used for Second
Cause → Effect Fire : Burn First causes Second
Action → Agent Write : Author First is done by Second
Degree of intensity Angry : Furious Second is extreme degree of First
Antonym Zenith : Nadir First and Second are opposites
Synonym Brave : Courageous First and Second mean the same
Worker → Product Author : Novel First creates Second
Worker → Workplace Doctor : Hospital First works in Second
Animal → Young Cat : Kitten Second is offspring of First
Animal → Sound Dog : Bark Second is sound made by First
Male → Female Lion : Lioness Second is female of First

Q1. PAINTER : CANVAS :: SCULPTOR : ?
ABrush
BMarble
CMuseum
DChisel
✅ B — A painter works on canvas; a sculptor works on marble. (Worker → Material relationship)
Q2. PROVOKE : PACIFY :: AGGRAVATE : ?

⚡ Solving Trick
Make a clear sentence: "A PAINTER works on a CANVAS in the same way that a SCULPTOR works on ___." Fill in the blank using your sentence structure. This prevents you from being misled by superficially similar options.
■ Verbal

Sentence Completion & Fill in the Blanks

Strategy for Sentence Completion

1
Read the full sentence — identify the overall meaning and tone before looking at options.
2
Look for signal words:
• Contrast signals: but, however, although, yet, despite, whereas, nevertheless → opposite idea follows
• Support signals: and, also, moreover, furthermore, in addition → similar idea follows
• Cause signals: because, since, as, therefore, thus, consequently
3
Predict the answer before looking at options. Use your prediction to eliminate wrong answers quickly.
4
Check if the answer makes sense by reading the completed sentence aloud in your head.
Q: Despite his reputation for being _______, the professor's lecture today was surprisingly engaging and full of fascinating anecdotes.

(Signal word: "Despite" → contrast → blank must mean NOT engaging)
ABrilliant
BTedious
CArticulate
DEnthusiastic
✅ B — "Despite" signals contrast. The lecture was engaging, so his reputation must be the OPPOSITE → tedious (boring/dull).

Double Blank Sentences

When two blanks appear, use the relationship between blanks as an extra clue:

Q: The scientist's theory was so _______ that it _______ even the most knowledgeable experts in the field.
Acomplex — simplified
Brevolutionary — bewildered
Csimple — confused
Dordinary — impressed
✅ B — "Even the most knowledgeable experts" were affected, implying the theory was beyond their understanding. Revolutionary theory → bewildered experts. The blanks must support each other.
■ Verbal

Error Spotting & Sentence Correction

Questions present a sentence divided into parts. Identify the part containing a grammatical error. If there's no error, mark "No Error."

The Most Common Error Types

Error Type Rule Wrong Correct
Subject-Verb Agreement Singular subject → singular verb; plural subject → plural verb The team are playing well The team is playing well
Collective Nouns Collective nouns take singular verb: team, jury, committee, group, family The jury were divided The jury was divided
Either/Neither + Verb Either…or / Neither…nor → verb agrees with the NEARER subject Either Ram or his brothers is wrong Either Ram or his brothers are wrong
Wrong Tense Consistent tense throughout a sentence He came in and sits down He came in and sat down
Pronoun Agreement Pronoun must agree with its antecedent in gender, number, person Everyone must do their best Everyone must do his/her best
Double Negative Two negatives make a positive — avoid in formal English I don't know nothing about it I know nothing about it
Misplaced Modifier Modifier must be placed next to the word it modifies She only drinks water in the morning She drinks only water in the morning
Preposition Error Different prepositions change meaning: interested IN, different FROM, similar TO She is different than me She is different from me
Use of "Less vs Fewer" Fewer = countable; Less = uncountable Less students came today Fewer students came today
Use of "Between vs Among" Between = two; Among = more than two Divide this between the five members Divide this among the five members

Sample Error Spotting

Q: (A) Neither the manager / (B) nor the employees / (C) were present / (D) at the meeting. / (E) No Error
Cwere present
✅ C — "Neither…nor" → verb agrees with the NEARER subject "employees" (plural). But wait — "employees" IS plural, so "were" is correct! Actually (E) No Error. In this construction: Neither…nor + plural nearest = plural verb. Trick: if the question says E is the answer, verify carefully!
⚡ Top Error Spotting Trick
Mentally break the sentence at commas and check each clause independently. Most errors occur in:
1. Subject-verb agreement (50% of errors)
2. Pronoun reference (20%)
3. Tense consistency (20%)
4. Preposition / article usage (10%)

Para Jumbles & Para Completion

Strategy to Crack Para Jumbles

1
Find the Opening Sentence: It introduces the topic without pronouns referring to unmentioned entities. Never has "however," "but," "therefore," or "also" at the start. Usually has the broadest, most general statement.
2
Find the Closing Sentence: Summarises, concludes, or gives a future outlook. Often has words like "thus," "finally," "in conclusion," "therefore." May contain the main solution or result.
3
Use Pronoun Clues: "He," "she," "it," "they," "this," "these," "those" always refer to something mentioned in the PREVIOUS sentence. Find the sentence that introduces the noun each pronoun refers to.
4
Use Connector Words: Look for logical connectors: "however," "moreover," "in addition," "consequently," "nevertheless," "on the other hand" — these link ideas in a predictable order.
5
Build Chains: Find 2–3 sentences that clearly follow each other. Use these chains to determine the overall order.
💡 Mandatory Pair technique: Find any two sentences that MUST go together (A always precedes B). Use this fixed relationship as an anchor to determine the options that work.

Para Completion Strategy

Given a paragraph with the last sentence missing — choose the best concluding line:

  • The concluding sentence must follow logically from the entire paragraph
  • It should match the tone of the passage (formal/informal, positive/negative)
  • It should NOT introduce an entirely new idea not discussed in the passage
  • Avoid options that are too specific (contradicts the generality of a conclusion) or too broad
■ Verbal

Critical Reasoning

Critical reasoning tests your ability to evaluate arguments — finding assumptions, strengths, weaknesses, conclusions, and inferences.

Key CR Question Types

Question Type What to Find Strategy
Conclusion What must be true if the argument is valid? The conclusion follows NECESSARILY from the premises. Should not require additional assumptions.
Assumption Unstated premise the argument depends on Use the Negation Test: negate each option. If negation DESTROYS the argument → that's the assumption.
Strengthen Which option makes the argument more valid? Find what would provide additional evidence supporting the conclusion.
Weaken Which option undermines the argument? Find what would make the conclusion less likely to be true.
Flaw What is wrong with the reasoning? Look for: hasty generalisation, false causation, circular reasoning, false dichotomy, ad hominem.
Inference What can be inferred from the passage? Must be directly supported. Avoid "too strong" or "beyond scope" options.

The Negation Test (for Assumptions)

Argument: "All students who scored above 90% in math will be selected for the advanced programme. Riya scored 94%. Therefore, Riya will be selected."

Q: Which of the following is an assumption?
ARiya studied very hard for her math exam
BNo student who scored above 90% will be excluded from the programme for any other reason
CThe advanced programme is beneficial for students
DRiya wants to join the advanced programme
✅ B — Negate B: "Students above 90% CAN be excluded for other reasons." If true, this DESTROYS the conclusion "Riya WILL be selected." So B is the assumption. Negating A, C, or D doesn't affect the argument.

Series questions ask you to identify the pattern and find the next/missing term. Over 80% of series follow one of these patterns.

Number Series — Pattern Types

Pattern Type Example How to Identify

Cubes 1, 8, 27, 64, 125 Terms are 1³, 2³, 3³...
Fibonacci-type 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21 Each term = sum of two preceding terms

Mixed operations 3, 7, 15, 31, 63 ×2+1 each time: 3→7(×2+1)→15(×2+1)→31...

Type Example Pattern

■ Non-Verbal

Figure / Non-Verbal Analogy

Figure analogy presents two related figures, then a third figure — find the fourth that maintains the same relationship.

What Changes Between Figures — Checklist

Shape Properties

Number of sides, shape type (triangle→square), curved vs angular, internal lines, symmetry

Size & Orientation

Rotation (90°, 180°, 270°), reflection, scaling (bigger/smaller), direction facing

Shading & Fill

Filled vs hollow, shading pattern (dots, lines, cross-hatch), which portion is shaded

Number & Count

Number of elements, how elements are arranged, elements added or removed

Position

Inside/outside, overlapping, which figure is on top (z-order), corner positions

Combination

Often 2–3 changes happen simultaneously — find ALL of them, not just the most obvious one

⚡ Systematic Approach
List all visible properties of Figure 1, then list what changed to get Figure 2. Apply EXACTLY those same changes to Figure 3 to predict Figure 4. Eliminate any option that differs from your prediction on ANY property.

■ Non-Verbal

Mirror & Water Images

The Core Rules

Mirror Image (Left-Right Reflection)

  • Mirror placed on the RIGHT side → image appears on the right
  • Left and right are SWAPPED, top and bottom remain the same
  • What faces left → faces right in the mirror
  • Number of elements, size, and up-down position: UNCHANGED
  • Trick for letters: d↔b, p↔q, reversed: E→Ǝ, F→⊣

Water Image (Top-Bottom Reflection)

  • Mirror placed at the BOTTOM → reflection below the object
  • Top and bottom are SWAPPED, left and right remain the same
  • What faces up → faces down in the water image
  • An upside-down version of the original
  • Trick for letters: n↔u, d↔p, M↔W, T→⊥
Q: If the word DOMAIN is placed in front of a vertical mirror, what will its reflection show?
ADOMAIN (unchanged)
BNIAMOB
CᴎIAMOᴅ (letters reversed left-right)
DUpside-down DOMAIN
✅ C — Mirror image reverses left and right. The word appears reversed, with each letter also flipped horizontally. Key: left-right swap, top-bottom preserved. D would be a water image.
⚡ Clock Mirror Trick
If a clock shows a time in a mirror, to find the real time: subtract the mirror-image time from 11:60. Mirror shows 7:20 → Real time = 11:60 − 7:20 = 4:40. This formula works for all clock mirror questions.
■ Non-Verbal

Paper Folding & Cutting

Rules for Paper Folding

  • Each fold doubles the layers. After 1 fold: 2 layers. After 2 folds: 4 layers. After n folds: 2ⁿ layers.
  • A hole punched through n layers creates 2ⁿ holes when unfolded (if no overlap)
  • The holes are symmetric about the fold line(s)
  • Holes near the fold line create symmetrical pairs on either side
  • The position of holes mirrors across each fold line

Step-by-Step Approach

1
Identify the fold lines — note direction (horizontal/vertical/diagonal) and order of folds.
2
Mentally unfold in REVERSE order — the last fold is unfolded first. Reflect the cut positions across each fold line.
3
Count total holes — multiply by 2 for each fold. One punch through 2-fold paper = 4 holes.
4
Check symmetry — the final answer must be symmetrical about all fold lines used.
🔑 Key insight: When a square paper is folded diagonally and a hole is punched at the corner (fold point), unfolding gives only 1 hole (the fold point). When punched away from the fold, you get 2 holes (one mirrored across the fold). A vertical + horizontal fold with centre punch = 1 hole (all layers overlap at centre).

Cubes & Dice

Cube Painting Rules

Cube Cut into n×n×n No faces painted 1 face painted 2 faces painted 3 faces painted
n=3 (27 small cubes) (n−2)³ = 1 6(n−2)² = 6 12(n−2) = 12 8 (always corners)
n=4 (64 small cubes) 8 24 24 8
n=5 (125 small cubes) 27 54 36 8
General formula (n×n×n) (n−2)³ 6(n−2)² 12(n−2) 8 (always 8)

Dice Rules — Opposite Faces

Standard Dice

On a standard die, opposite faces always sum to 7:
• 1 is opposite to 6
• 2 is opposite to 5
• 3 is opposite to 4

Finding Opposite Faces from Diagrams

When two positions of a die are shown, the faces visible in both positions cannot be opposite. The common faces help eliminate options. Any face NOT appearing in either view is opposite to the face common to both views.

⚡ Dice Trick — Common Face Method
Given two views of a die: (1) Find a face common to both views. (2) The face opposite to the common face is the one that appears in neither view. (3) Then use rotation to determine the remaining pairs. Works for 90% of dice questions.
■ Non-Verbal

Venn Diagrams

Venn diagram questions in aptitude tests either: (A) ask what shape best represents the relationship between three groups, OR (B) give numbers in regions and ask you to calculate totals.

Common Venn Diagram Formulas

n(A ∪ B) = n(A) + n(B) − n(A ∩ B)
n(A ∪ B ∪ C) = n(A) + n(B) + n(C) − n(A∩B) − n(B∩C) − n(A∩C) + n(A∩B∩C)
Only A = n(A) − n(A∩B) − n(A∩C) + n(A∩B∩C)
Q: In a class of 60 students, 30 play Cricket, 25 play Football, and 10 play both. How many play neither?
A10
B13
C15
D20
✅ C — n(C∪F) = 30 + 25 − 10 = 45. Play neither = 60 − 45 = 15.

Directions & Distances

The Compass Rose — Direction Rules

Basic Directions

   N
NW ↖ ↑ ↗ NE
W ← • → E
SW ↙ ↓ ↘ SE

Turning Rules

  • Facing North + Right turn = East
  • Facing North + Left turn = West
  • Facing East + Right turn = South
  • Facing East + Left turn = North
  • 180° turn = opposite direction always
  • 4 × 90° turns = back to start direction
⚡ Shadow Direction Trick
In the MORNING (sunrise in East): If person faces North, shadow falls WEST. If faces South, shadow falls EAST. In the EVENING (sunset in West): If person faces North, shadow falls EAST. Formula: shadow is ALWAYS on opposite side from sun. Draw a simple diagram — it takes 10 seconds and prevents errors.

■ Logical

Seating Arrangement

Linear vs Circular Arrangement

Linear Arrangement

  • People sit in a row facing one direction (usually North/South)
  • "Left" and "Right" are absolute — fix them from the start
  • Place the most constrained person first (most clues about them)
  • Fix 1–2 people whose positions are certain, then place others relative to them

Circular Arrangement

  • People sit around a circular table — fix ONE person first (they are the reference)
  • Facing the centre: right = clockwise, left = anti-clockwise
  • Facing away from centre: right = anti-clockwise, left = clockwise
  • "Opposite" in circular = n/2 seats away (for n people)

Strategy for Arrangement Questions

1
Count people and seats — 6 people, 6 chairs in a line / circle. Draw the structure first.
2
Identify definite positions first — "A sits at one end" → place A at position 1. Build from certainties.
3
Apply "next to" and "not next to" constraints — Use trial and error when two options remain. Try one; if contradiction arises, try the other.
4
Verify every clue — Once all positions are filled, re-check ALL given clues. If any is violated, backtrack.
■ Logical

Syllogisms

Syllogisms give two or more statements and ask which conclusions follow. Always treat statements as TRUE regardless of real-world logic.

Venn Diagram Approach for Syllogisms

Statement Type Example Venn Representation
All A are B (Universal Positive) All cats are animals Circle A entirely inside Circle B
No A is B (Universal Negative) No dog is a cat Circles A and B completely separate
Some A are B (Particular Positive) Some fruits are vegetables Circles A and B partially overlapping
Some A are not B (Particular Negative) Some birds cannot fly Part of A outside B
Statements:
1. All mangoes are fruits.
2. All fruits are sweet.

Conclusions:
I. All mangoes are sweet.
II. Some sweets are mangoes.
AOnly I follows
BBoth I and II follow
COnly II follows
DNeither follows
✅ B — Mangoes⊂Fruits⊂Sweet. So: All mangoes are sweet (I ✓). If all mangoes are sweet, then some sweets are mangoes (II ✓ — conversion of "All mangoes are sweet" → "Some sweets are mangoes").
⚡ Syllogism Shortcut Rules
• All A are B + All B are C → All A are C ✓
• All A are B + No B is C → No A is C ✓
• All A are B + Some B are C → Some A are C ✗ (NOT necessarily)
• Some A are B + Some B are C → No definite conclusion about A and C
• "Some A are B" can always be converted to "Some B are A" ✓

Type Definition Key Test

Weak Argument Vague, trivial, based on assumptions, or not directly related "It has always been done this way" — tradition-based = always weak

Does Not Follow Requires assumptions or outside information If you need world knowledge not in the text → doesn't follow

CAll are strong
DNone is strong

Two Articles — Tricky Scenario

⚡ Equal SP, Same Profit% and Loss% → Always a Loss!
If two items are sold at the same SP, one at x% profit and one at x% loss, the net result is always a LOSS of:

Permutation, Combination & Probability

Core Formulas

Permutation (order matters)
ⁿPr = n! / (n−r)!
Combination (order doesn't matter)
ⁿCr = n! / [r! × (n−r)!]
Circular permutation
(n−1)! ways (fix one person)
Probability
P(E) = Favourable outcomes / Total outcomes
P(A or B)
P(A∪B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A∩B)
P(A and B) — independent
P(A∩B) = P(A) × P(B)
Complement
P(not A) = 1 − P(A)
Conditional probability
P(A|B) = P(A∩B) / P(B)

P vs C — Quick Decide

  • Use P (Permutation) when order MATTERS: passwords, phone numbers, seating in a row, medals (gold/silver/bronze)
  • Use C (Combination) when order DOESN'T matter: committee selection, choosing a team, handshakes, groupings
💡 Trick: ⁿCr = ⁿC(n-r). So ¹⁰C₇ = ¹⁰C₃ = 120. Always compute the smaller of r and (n-r) for faster calculation.

1
