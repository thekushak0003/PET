■ Foundations

What is a Case Study Interview?

A case study interview presents a real or hypothetical business problem and asks you to analyse it, structure your thinking, and deliver a recommendation — all under pressure, in real time.

Why Companies Use Case Studies

Simulate Real Work

Consulting and strategy work is exactly this: ambiguous problems → structured analysis → actionable recommendations. Cases test if you can actually do the job.

Test Structured Thinking

Can you break a complex, messy problem into MECE components? Can you identify what matters most and ignore what doesn't?

Assess Communication

Can you articulate complex ideas clearly? Do you think aloud confidently? Can you handle pushback without crumbling?

What is Actually Being Evaluated

Dimension What Interviewers Look For Weight
Problem Structuring MECE breakdown, issue trees, logical hypothesis, not jumping to answers High
Analytical Rigour Correct maths, reasonable assumptions, data-driven reasoning High
Business Judgement Common sense, prioritisation, understanding of business context High
Communication Clear, confident, concise; structured answers; good listening High
Hypothesis-driven Forming a view early and testing it vs boiling the ocean Medium
Creativity Generating non-obvious insights and solutions Medium
Grace Under Pressure Staying calm, recovering from mistakes, handling curveballs Medium
Synthesis Pulling scattered findings into a crisp, actionable recommendation High

Where Case Studies Appear

Management Consulting

McKinsey, BCG, Bain, Deloitte, KPMG, EY, PwC — core selection method at all rounds.

Strategy Roles

Corporate strategy, business development, chief of staff, strategy & operations at tech firms (Google, Meta, Amazon).

Product Management

PM interviews include product strategy cases, market sizing, and feature prioritisation cases.

Investment Banking & PE

Modelling cases, LBO scenarios, deal analysis. More quantitative than consulting cases.

💡 Key insight: The interviewer doesn't expect a perfect answer. They want to see a structured mind at work. A wrong answer with great structure beats a right answer with no structure every time.

Case Formats

Interviewer-led (Traditional)

Interviewer guides the case, provides data on request, answers clarifying questions. 30–45 mins. Most common at MBB (McKinsey, BCG, Bain).

Candidate-led

You drive entirely — ask for data you need, set the agenda. Tests initiative and structure. BCG, Deloitte often use this style.

Written / Take-home Case

Given a pack of data (exhibits, tables). 1–3 hours. Produce a structured written analysis and slides. Tests depth, not just verbal structure.

Group Case

2–4 candidates tackle a case together while assessors observe. Tests collaboration, leadership, constructive disagreement, and listening.

Video / AI-led Case

Recorded or AI-moderated. No real-time feedback. Tests self-structure and comfort with ambiguity alone. McKinsey's Solve Game.

Industry / Technical Case

Domain-specific (e.g., healthcare, manufacturing). Tests industry knowledge + consulting skills. Common at strategy boutiques.

The Case Study Mindset

The mindset you bring into the room matters as much as the frameworks you've memorised. These mental models separate good candidates from exceptional ones.

The Four Core Mental Models

Think Like a Consultant
🔬
Hypothesis-first, not data-first
Form a view early. "My initial hypothesis is X because of Y. I'll test it by examining Z." Don't wait for all the data — drive toward an answer and update as you learn.
🗺️
Breadth before depth (MECE)
Always sketch the full problem space before diving into any single branch. Premature depth = missing the real issue hiding in the other branch.
🎯
Prioritise ruthlessly (80/20)
You have 30 minutes, not 3 weeks. Identify the 2–3 issues most likely to contain the answer. Explicitly say: "I'll focus on X because it's likely the biggest driver."
💡
Answer first (Pyramid Principle)
Lead with the recommendation, follow with evidence. Never build up to the answer — give it first. "I recommend X. Here's why: 1, 2, 3."

Thinking Out Loud

Unlike exams, the interviewer scores your thinking process, not just your conclusion. Narrate your thought process continuously:

✅ DO say

  • "Let me structure this — I'm thinking about it as two buckets: revenue and costs…"
  • "Before I dive in, can I take 60 seconds to sketch a framework?"
  • "My hypothesis is X. To test it, I'd want to know…"
  • "I'm going to focus on the revenue side first because it's likely the bigger driver."
  • "That's interesting — it changes my hypothesis. Let me update my thinking."

❌ DON'T do

  • Go silent for 30+ seconds without explaining what you're doing
  • Jump straight to a solution without structuring
  • Say "I don't know" and stop — always say "I'd estimate X because…"
  • Change your answer the moment the interviewer pushes back
  • Apologise excessively when you make a maths error

The Mindset Shift: "So What?"

After every piece of analysis, ask yourself: "So what does this mean for the client?" Every insight must connect to an implication or action. Raw data without interpretation is noise.

So What? Chain
📊 Data: Revenue fell 15%
→
🔍 Insight: All decline from one product line
→
💡 Implication: Problem is isolated, not systemic
→
🎯 Action: Focus intervention on that product line
■ Structure & Approach

The Universal Case Structure

Every case interview — regardless of type — follows the same high-level arc. Internalise this so deeply it becomes automatic.

The 6-Phase Arc

1
Open: Listen & confirm (1–2 min)
Receive the prompt. Listen carefully. Take notes. Repeat the question back in your own words to confirm understanding and show active listening. "So if I understand correctly, your client is X, facing Y, and you'd like me to recommend Z — is that right?"
2
Clarify: Ask 1–2 targeted questions (1–2 min)
Ask only high-value clarifying questions. "What does success look like?" "Is this a short-term fix or a long-term strategy?" "Any parts of the business that are out of scope?" Don't ask for information you should be able to derive yourself.
3
Structure: Build your framework (2–3 min)
"I'd like to take 60–90 seconds to structure my thinking." Build a MECE issue tree on paper. Announce the structure: "I'm going to look at this through three lenses: X, Y, and Z. I'll start with X because it's likely the biggest driver."
4
Analyse: Work through branches (15–20 min)
Proceed methodically. Ask for data when needed. Do mental maths out loud. Form mini-hypotheses per branch. Signpost clearly: "Moving to the cost side now…" Prioritise the 2–3 branches most likely to hold the answer.
5
Synthesise: Pull findings together (2–3 min)
"Based on what we've covered, let me synthesise." Draw connections between findings. Identify the root cause. Frame as: "The problem is fundamentally about X, driven primarily by Y."
6
Recommend: Crisp, direct recommendation (1–2 min)
"I recommend that the client does X." Give 2–3 supporting reasons. Acknowledge the key risk and a mitigation. Propose clear next steps. Never be wishy-washy.

The Recommendation Template

Final Recommendation Structure
1. The Headline
"Based on our analysis, I recommend that [client] should [specific action]."
2. Supporting Reasons (2–3)
"The primary reason is [Reason 1], supported by [evidence]. Additionally, [Reason 2] which is validated by [data]. Finally, [Reason 3]."
3. Key Risk + Mitigation
"The most significant risk is [Risk], which we can mitigate by [specific action / condition]."
4. Next Steps (3 prioritised)
"If we proceed, the immediate next steps would be: (1) [quick win], (2) [medium-term action], (3) [long-term investment]."
💡 One-sentence test: Can you summarise your recommendation in one sentence? If not, it's not clear enough yet. The best recommendations pass this test: "The client should exit the European market and redeploy capital into APAC over 18 months."
■ Structure & Approach

Clarifying Questions — What to Ask & When

The questions you ask in the first 2 minutes reveal your business acumen and problem-solving instincts more than almost anything else in the case.

The Four Types of Clarifying Questions

Category Question Why It Matters
Objective "What does success look like for the client in this situation?" Defines the end goal — prevents solving the wrong problem
Objective "Is there a specific financial target or timeline we're working toward?" Anchors the scope of your analysis
Scope "Are there any areas of the business or geographies that are out of scope?" Saves time on irrelevant analysis
Context "Has this problem occurred before or is this a new trend?" Immediately suggests whether it's cyclical or structural
Context "Is the industry experiencing this challenge broadly or is it specific to our client?" Determines if external or internal cause
Constraints "Are there any financial, regulatory, or strategic constraints we should be aware of?" Prevents recommending options that are already ruled out
Definition "When you say 'profitability,' are we talking gross margin, EBITDA, or net profit?" Ensures you're measuring the right thing

What NOT to Ask

⚠️ Don't ask questions you could answer yourself or that reveal a lack of business knowledge. "What is EBITDA?" or "Can you explain what a supply chain is?" will immediately signal you're not ready. If something is truly unclear, frame it as a confirmation: "Just to confirm — when you say 'growth,' you mean top-line revenue growth, correct?"

Reading Between the Lines

Often the most important information is what the interviewer didn't say. Train yourself to notice:

  • Time specificity — "profits fell last quarter" → look for recent events (new competitor? cost spike? pricing change?)
  • Comparison language — "our margins are lower than industry" → benchmark problem, not absolute problem
  • Emotional language — "the CEO is very concerned" → urgency, political dimensions, perhaps a board presentation is coming
  • Passive voice — "costs have risen" vs "we increased our headcount" → internal vs external cause already implied

Choosing & Adapting Frameworks

Frameworks are starting points — not straitjackets. The best candidates adapt standard frameworks to the specific case rather than mechanically applying them.

Framework Selection Guide

Case Type Primary Framework Supporting Tools
Profitability Profit = Revenue − Cost tree (Price × Volume; Fixed + Variable) Benchmarking, Porter's 5 Forces, Value Chain
Market Entry 3Cs: Market (customers, size, growth) → Competition → Company capabilities Ansoff Matrix, Porter's 5 Forces, Entry modes
Growth Strategy Ansoff Matrix (existing/new × product/market) Revenue tree, Porter's, Customer segmentation
M&A / Investment Strategic fit → Target quality → Synergies → Risks → Valuation Porter's, DCF basics, Integration considerations
Pricing 3 lenses: WTP (value-based) + Cost floor + Competitive anchor Elasticity, price-volume trade-off, segmentation
Operations Value Chain → Process mapping → Root cause analysis Lean waste types, benchmarking, capacity analysis
Competitive Response Porter's 5 Forces + Competitor analysis SWOT, blue ocean, differentiation options
Market Sizing Top-down (population funnel) OR Bottom-up (unit × price) Triangulate both; sanity check against known anchors

Building a Custom Framework

For unusual cases, build a bespoke framework by asking: "What are the 3–4 most important categories of issues that could explain this problem?"

Example: "A hospital is losing patients to competitors"

Not a standard profitability case — needs a custom lens:

Patient Experience
Wait times, service quality, communication, post-care follow-up
Clinical Quality
Outcomes, specialist availability, equipment, accreditations
Access & Location
Transport links, appointment availability, digital booking, insurance accepted
Competitor Moves
New entrant? Competitor improvement? Changed referral patterns?
⚠️ The "framework trap": Blindly applying Porter's 5 Forces to every case, or starting every answer with "I'd like to look at this through a SWOT lens" signals you're relying on memorised templates rather than thinking. Frameworks should emerge from the problem, not be imposed on it.

Synthesis & Recommendation

Synthesis is where most candidates stumble. They can analyse, but they can't connect the pieces into a coherent story. This skill separates good from great.

What is Synthesis?

Synthesis is not a summary. It's the act of drawing connections between disparate findings to arrive at a root cause and a direction for action.

Summary (wrong)

"Revenue fell 12%, costs rose 8%, margins dropped from 18% to 6%, and the new competitor has 15% market share."

Just restating findings — no value added.

Synthesis (right)

"The margin collapse is fundamentally a revenue problem — the new low-cost competitor has forced us into defensive price cuts that are not supported by our cost structure. This tells us we need to either differentiate upward or restructure costs."

Findings connected → Root cause → Direction.

The Issue Tree to Synthesis Flow

Build Issue Tree
MECE branches of potential causes
→
Gather Evidence
Data per branch; confirm or rule out
→
Identify Root Cause
Which branch explains most of the variance?
→
Draw Implications
"This means we should / should not…"
→
Recommend
Specific, prioritised, actionable

Handling Pushback

Interviewers often push back on your recommendation — even if it's correct — to see how you handle pressure. The right response is not to immediately capitulate.

Pushback Response Framework
Step 1: Acknowledge
"That's a really important point / fair challenge — thank you for raising it."
Step 2: Evaluate honestly
Ask yourself: Is this pushback based on new information I hadn't considered? Or is it a test of my conviction?
Step 3a: If they're right — update
"You're right — I hadn't fully weighted [factor]. That actually changes my view. I'd now lean toward [revised recommendation] because…"
Step 3b: If you're right — hold firm
"I understand the concern, and it's a valid risk. However, I still recommend X because [evidence A] and [evidence B]. The downside of not acting seems greater than the risk you've identified."
💡 The key: Change your mind when the evidence warrants it. Don't change your mind just because the interviewer looks sceptical. Intellectual honesty + conviction under pressure = the ideal candidate.

Step-by-Step Profitability Diagnosis

1
Scope it: Is this a single product, division, or whole company? Which geography? When did it start? Is it getting worse or stabilising?
2
Benchmark: Is the industry profitable? If yes → company-specific issue. If no → industry-wide (macro, structural, competitive).
3
Isolate revenue vs cost: If revenue is flat/up but profit fell → cost problem. If revenue fell → dig into price vs volume.
4
Drill down: Once you know it's e.g., a variable cost issue, drill: which cost line? Is it one-off or recurring? Internal decision or external factor?
5
Root cause → fix: Match the root cause to the right lever. Revenue fix: pricing strategy, sales force, new segments. Cost fix: renegotiate contracts, automate, outsource.

Market Entry Cases

The Three Core Questions

1. Is the Market Attractive?

Size, growth rate, profitability of incumbents, competition intensity (Porter's 5), regulation, customer needs, barriers to entry.

2. Can We Compete and Win?

Do we have the capabilities, brand, relationships, cost structure, and technology? What's our advantage vs. incumbents? Right to win?

3. Is the Return Worth It?

Investment required, timeline to profitability, expected market share, risk-adjusted return vs alternatives (opportunity cost).

Entry Mode Comparison

Mode Investment Control Speed Risk Best When
Organic / Greenfield High Full Slow High No good acquisition target; want full control; time not critical
Acquisition Very High Full Fast Very High Need speed; target has capabilities/customers/distribution
Joint Venture Medium Shared Medium Medium Need local knowledge; regulatory requirement; risk-sharing
Licensing / Franchise Low Low Fast Low Limited capital; scalable model; partner has distribution
Strategic Partnership Low Minimal Fastest Lowest Test market; access without full commitment

Growth Strategy Cases

Revenue Growth Levers

Growth = Price × Volume — expanded
Existing Customers
  • Increase purchase frequency
  • Increase average order value (upsell/cross-sell)
  • Reduce churn / improve retention
  • Price increase (if WTP allows)
  • Win back lapsed customers
New Customers
  • Expand to new geographies
  • Target new customer segments
  • New channels (online, partnerships, D2C)
  • Referral / viral growth programmes
  • Bundling with complementary products

The Ansoff Matrix Applied

Strategy Products Markets Risk Example Levers
Market Penetration Existing Existing Lowest Price cuts, promotions, loyalty schemes, more sales reps
Market Development Existing New Medium Geographic expansion, new demographics, new channels
Product Development New Existing Medium Line extensions, complementary products, new features
Diversification New New Highest M&A, joint ventures, entirely new business units
💡 Start with penetration: Unless the market is saturated, the lowest-risk growth strategy is always penetration — selling more to existing customers through existing channels. Only recommend diversification if all lower-risk options are exhausted.

M&A / Investment Cases

The M&A Value Equation

Core Logic
Value Created = PV(Synergies) − Acquisition Premium

A deal only creates value if the present value of synergies exceeds the premium paid above market value

The 6-Step M&A Framework

1
Strategic Rationale — Why this deal? Horizontal consolidation, vertical integration, new capability acquisition, geographic expansion, acqui-hire, defensive blocking?
2
Target Quality — Is the target a good business? Revenue growth, margins, competitive moat, customer quality, management team, IP/brand.
3
Synergy Quantification — Revenue synergies (cross-sell, new markets) and cost synergies (shared overheads, procurement). When do they materialise? What % are achievable?
4
Risks & Red Flags — Cultural mismatch, regulatory approval risk, customer/talent attrition, hidden liabilities, integration complexity, leverage risk.
5
Valuation & Affordability — Is the price reasonable? Compare EV/EBITDA multiple to sector peers. Can the acquirer afford it without compromising their own strategy?
6
Integration Plan — Day 1 priorities, systems integration, culture integration, leadership decisions, customer communication, timeline to synergy realisation.

Pricing Cases

The Pricing Triangle

Customer WTP (Ceiling)

Maximum any customer will pay. Determined by the economic value created for them. Survey, conjoint analysis, or competitive benchmarking reveal WTP.

Cost (Floor)

Variable cost at minimum; full cost + required margin for sustainability. Price must always exceed variable cost or each unit sold is a loss.

Competitive Anchor (Context)

What substitutes cost shapes customer expectations. Price in relation to alternatives — even for a novel product.

Pricing Strategy Matrix

Strategy Set price based on… Use when… Risk
Value-based Customer's economic value gained Differentiated product with clear ROI for customer Hard to measure WTP precisely
Cost-plus Cost × (1 + margin%) Commodities, government contracts, simple pricing Ignores market — may over- or underprice
Competitive Competitor prices as anchor Commodity markets, price-sensitive segments Can trigger price wars; only works if costs lower
Penetration Below market to gain share New entrant, network effects, rapid adoption needed Hard to raise price later; signals low quality
Price skimming High launch price, reduce over time Novel product, no substitutes, early adopters with high WTP Invites competition; early adopters feel exploited
Freemium Free tier + premium tier Digital products with low marginal cost; network effects Low conversion; free tier is expensive to serve

Operational Cases

The Ops Diagnosis Framework

1
Map the value chain — Where does this business create value? Which step is the bottleneck? (Inbound logistics → Operations → Outbound → Marketing → Service)
2
Identify the constraint — Where does work pile up? Where are cycle times longest? Where is utilisation highest? (Theory of Constraints: the weakest link limits the whole system)
3
Root cause analysis — People, Process, Technology, or Materials? Use "5 Whys" to dig beneath surface symptoms to structural causes.
4
Benchmark — How does this metric compare to best-in-class? If industry standard cycle time is 3 days and we're at 12 days → 4× gap to close.
5
Prioritise interventions — Quick wins (0–3 months, low investment) vs medium-term (process redesign) vs long-term (technology investment, new facilities).

Key Operational Metrics

Metric Formula Interpretation
Capacity Utilisation Actual output ÷ Maximum output >85% = potential bottleneck; <60% = underutilised asset
Overall Equipment Effectiveness (OEE) Availability × Performance × Quality World class = 85%+; most firms operate at 40–60%
Cycle Time End time − Start time per unit Lower = more throughput; compare to industry benchmark
Defect Rate (DPMO) Defects per million opportunities Six Sigma target: 3.4 DPMO
Inventory Turnover COGS ÷ Average Inventory Higher = leaner; low = cash tied up in stock
On-time Delivery (OTD) On-time deliveries ÷ Total deliveries Best-in-class: 95%+
■ Quantitative Skills

Fermi Estimation & Market Sizing

Fermi estimation is the art of making reasonable estimates with limited data using structured logic. It is tested in every consulting interview in some form.

The Two Approaches

Top-Down (Demand-side)

Start with the total population → filter down to the relevant segment → estimate penetration → multiply by value.


Example: Market for gym memberships in Delhi

Delhi population: 20M
Adults (60%): 12M
Disposable income bracket (30%): 3.6M
Health-conscious (25%): 0.9M
Currently gym members (40%): 360,000
Avg annual fee: ₹15,000
Market = ₹540 Cr

Bottom-Up (Supply-side)

Start from supply units (stores, workers, capacity) → estimate usage per unit → multiply by price.


Example: Revenue of a busy restaurant per year

Seats: 50
Turns per day: 3 (lunch+dinner)
Occupancy: 70%
Daily covers: 50 × 3 × 0.7 = 105
Avg bill: ₹800
Operating days: 300/year
Revenue = ₹2.52 Cr / year

Key Reference Numbers to Memorise

Reference Point Approximate Value
India population 1.4 billion
Urban India (% of population) ~36% → ~500M people
Delhi / Mumbai / Bangalore population ~20M / 20M / 12M
India middle class (₹5–30L household income) ~100–150M people
India internet users ~900M (2024)
India smartphone users ~750M
Average Indian urban household size 4 people
Working days per year ~250 days
Hours per year 8,760 (~9,000)
India GDP (approximate) ~$3.5 trillion (2024)

Sanity Check Your Answer

Always do a sense-check: "Does this feel right for what I know about the world?" If your estimate says India's coffee market is larger than India's GDP — something is wrong. Common anchors:

  • India's total retail market: ~$900B
  • FMCG market in India: ~$110B
  • E-commerce market in India: ~$70B (growing fast)
  • A Tier-1 city coffee shop: ₹1–3 Cr revenue/year is reasonable

EBITDA Margin EBITDA ÷ Revenue Company profitability; typical for M&A valuation multiples

Rounding & Approximation

💡 Round aggressively then sanity-check. In case maths: use 300M instead of 290M, ₹50 instead of ₹47. State your rounding: "I'll use ₹500 average spend for simplicity." Interviewers want to see the logic, not perfect arithmetic. Accuracy within ±20% is almost always sufficient.
■ Worked Case · Profitability

Case 1: The Struggling Coffee Chain

☕
BrewPoint — National Coffee Chain (India)
Profitability Case · Difficulty: Medium · ~30 min
"Your client, BrewPoint, is a national coffee chain with 200 outlets across India. Despite strong revenue growth of 12% this year, their EBITDA margin has fallen from 18% to 9%. The CEO is alarmed and has asked your firm to find out why and what to do."
🎤 Step 1 — Clarifying Questions to Ask
  • Is the margin drop consistent across all outlets, or is it concentrated in specific geographies / formats?
  • When did the decline begin — was it sudden or gradual?
  • Is this decline affecting competitors too, or is it specific to BrewPoint?
  • Has there been any major change to the business — new store format, menu overhaul, acquisition?
Interviewer response: The margin drop started 8 months ago. It's across all outlets. Competitors appear to be doing fine. A new premium store format was launched 10 months ago.
🏗️ Step 2 — Framework
Since revenue grew but margin fell → most likely a cost problem, but also check mix shift. Frame as:
Revenue Analysis (mix shift?) → Cost Analysis (what drove cost up?) → Premium Store Format (culprit?)
📊 Step 3 — Analysis & Data
Revenue: ₹500 Cr → ₹560 Cr (+12%). Revenue per outlet: ₹280L → ₹285L (+1.8%). So revenue growth is mostly from new outlets.

Costs: EBITDA margin fell from 18% to 9% → EBITDA fell from ₹90 Cr to ₹50.4 Cr despite higher revenue. Costs rose from ₹410 Cr to ₹509.6 Cr (+24%)!

New format: 40 premium stores opened. Average capex ₹2 Cr each = ₹80 Cr total. Depreciation ≈ ₹8 Cr/year. These stores have 35% higher rent and 25% higher labour costs. But their revenue per outlet is only 18% higher than standard stores — cost premium isn't justified by revenue premium.
🎯 Step 4 — Recommendation
Root cause: The premium store format has a cost structure that outpaces its revenue premium. Fixed cost growth (rent + labour) driven by these 40 stores is the primary driver of margin collapse.

Recommend: (1) Pause premium store rollout immediately — run a 90-day performance review of existing 40 stores. (2) Renegotiate leases on worst-performing premium locations — exit 10–15 underperformers. (3) Redesign the premium format to reduce fixed cost intensity without losing brand positioning. (4) In parallel, investigate whether premium format revenue can be improved through menu pricing, loyalty programme, or B2B catering partnerships.

Case 2: Telecom Market Entry

📱
TelcoVision — European Operator considering Indian Entry
Market Entry Case · Difficulty: Hard · ~35 min
"TelcoVision is a large European telecom operator with no presence in Asia. They are considering entering the Indian mobile market. Should they enter, and if so, how?"
🏗️ Framework Applied
Use 3-part structure: Market Attractiveness → Competitive Position → Entry Strategy & Financials
📊 Market Attractiveness
Size: ~1.1B mobile subscribers. ~₹2.5 trillion market. 5G rollout underway — significant upgrade cycle ahead.
Growth: Subscriber growth slowing (near saturation) but ARPU growing as data usage explodes. Enterprise and IoT expanding fast.
Competition: Three major players — Jio (dominant, ~40% share), Airtel (premium, ~35%), Vi (struggling, ~20%). Intense price competition. Jio entered in 2016 with free data and permanently disrupted pricing. Margins are thin — Jio EBITDA margin ~52%, Airtel ~50%, Vi barely profitable.
Regulation: Spectrum auctions are expensive. Foreign direct investment cap requires Indian partner (26% equity allowed directly; 100% via automatic route for infrastructure). TRAI is active regulator.
⚔️ Competitive Position
TelcoVision has no brand recognition in India, no spectrum, no infrastructure, and no distribution. The market is a three-player oligopoly with massive incumbents who have sunk ₹4+ trillion in infrastructure. Entering the consumer market directly would require ₹2–3 trillion in investment with uncertain returns given Jio's pricing power.

However: The enterprise 5G market is underpenetrated. Multinationals operating in India need telecom partners who understand global enterprise needs. This is a niche TelcoVision could credibly serve.
🎯 Recommendation
Do NOT enter the consumer market. The economics are unfavourable, incumbents are deeply entrenched, and the capital required is prohibitive.

Enter via the enterprise segment as a virtual network operator (MVNO) or through a partnership with Airtel, leveraging TelcoVision's existing enterprise client relationships globally. Serve multinationals operating in India who need seamless global connectivity — a segment neither Jio nor Airtel prioritises. Investment required: ₹500–800 Cr vs ₹2+ trillion for full entry. Timeline to profitability: 3–4 years.
■ Worked Case · Growth + Pricing

Case 3: EV Start-up Pricing

🚗
Volta Motors — Indian Electric Two-Wheeler Start-up
Pricing + Growth Strategy · Difficulty: Medium · ~30 min
"Volta Motors is preparing to launch its first electric scooter in India. They've asked you to help them determine the right launch price and a go-to-market strategy."
📊 Key Data (provided by interviewer)
Variable cost per scooter: ₹70,000. Fixed costs: ₹50 Cr/year. Capacity: 20,000 scooters/year. Comparable ICE scooter (Honda Activa): ₹80,000. Ola Electric (competitor EV): ₹1,00,000. Ather Energy (premium EV): ₹1,40,000. Target market: Urban daily commuters (25–45 yrs), 100–150 km/day range requirement. Running cost: EV ≈ ₹0.25/km vs ICE ≈ ₹2.5/km. Average daily commute: 30 km.
🔢 Pricing Analysis
Cost floor: ₹70,000 variable + break-even requires: ₹50Cr ÷ 20,000 units = ₹25,000 fixed cost/unit → minimum price = ₹95,000.

WTP (value-based): EV saves ₹(2.5−0.25) × 30 km × 300 days = ₹20,250/year in fuel. Over 5 years: ₹1,01,250 savings. Plus lower maintenance ≈ ₹20,000 over 5 years. Total 5-year value = ₹1,21,000 over ICE. WTP ceiling = ICE price + value premium = ₹80,000 + ₹60,000 (customers rarely pay full value) = ₹1,40,000.

Competitive anchor: Ola at ₹1,00,000 is the reference. Differentiate on range and service network → justify ₹10,000–20,000 premium.

Recommended launch price: ₹1,10,000 — above cost floor (₹95K), below Ather premium tier, and clearly above Ola on differentiated specs. Margin = ₹40,000/unit. Break-even = ₹50Cr ÷ ₹40,000 = 12,500 units (63% of capacity).
🎯 Go-to-Market Strategy
Phase 1 (0–6 months): Launch in 3 Tier-1 cities (Delhi, Bengaluru, Pune) — highest EV awareness, best charging infrastructure. Direct-to-consumer via brand experience centres + digital. Target: 5,000 units.
Phase 2 (6–18 months): Expand to 10 Tier-2 cities. Add dealer network. EMI financing partnerships with banks.
Phase 3 (18–36 months): National rollout, fleet partnerships (delivery companies, ride-hailing), B2B segment.
■ Worked Case · Operations

Case 4: Hospital Operations

🏥
MediCore Hospital — Delhi NCR
Operations / Process Improvement · Difficulty: Hard · ~35 min
"MediCore is a 500-bed private hospital in Delhi NCR. Patient satisfaction scores have declined 20% over 18 months and occupancy is falling from 85% to 72%. The MD has asked for urgent help."
🏗️ Custom Framework
Not a standard profitability case. Build a custom structure: Patient Journey (pre-visit → during → post-visit) × Service Quality Drivers (Access, Clinical, Non-clinical, Administrative)
📊 Key Findings (from data provided)
Wait times: OPD wait up from 22 min to 58 min. 40% of patients wait >45 min. Post-consultation billing: 35 min average.
Clinical quality: No change in outcomes; retained all accreditations. No clinical complaints.
Staff: Nursing attrition 28% (vs 14% 2 years ago). High attrition → more agency nurses → less familiarity → slower workflows → longer wait times.
Competition: Two new multi-specialty hospitals opened within 5 km in the last 18 months. Both have digital appointment systems. MediCore still uses phone booking.
Patient feedback themes: "Long waits," "Can't book appointments online," "Hard to find parking," "Bills are confusing."
🔍 Root Cause
Two reinforcing problems: (1) Operational: High nursing attrition → agency reliance → workflow inefficiency → long waits. (2) Digital lag: No online booking while competitors offer it → convenience gap → patients switching for routine care. The clinical quality is not the issue; the experience and access are.
🎯 Prioritised Recommendations
Immediate (0–3 months): (1) Launch digital appointment booking — off-the-shelf solution, 6–8 weeks to implement. (2) Redesign OPD patient flow — dedicated fast lanes for follow-up visits (shorter wait), triage nurse at front. Expected: reduce wait time to <30 min.

Medium-term (3–9 months): Address nursing attrition — market benchmarking on compensation + improve scheduling flexibility + career development programme. Target: reduce attrition to <18%. Implement billing transparency — itemised bills with QR codes to explain charges.

Long-term (9–18 months): Add parking structure (key hygiene factor). Launch patient loyalty programme. Develop telemedicine for post-discharge follow-up — increases patient touchpoints without bed consumption.

Communication, Presence & Signposting

In case interviews, how you say something is nearly as important as what you say. Interviewers at top firms score communication independently from analysis.

Signposting — Your Verbal GPS

Signposting tells the interviewer where you are in your analysis and where you're going next. It creates structure in the listener's mind.

Signposting Phrases by Purpose
Starting structure
"I'd like to approach this through three lenses..."
"Before I dive in, let me frame the problem as..."
"I'm going to take 60 seconds to structure this."
Transitioning
"Now let me move to the cost side..."
"That covers revenue. Turning to costs..."
"Having looked at X, I now want to explore Y."
Requesting data
"To test this hypothesis, it would be helpful to know..."
"Do you have data on [X]?"
"My assumption is Y — does that seem reasonable?"
Synthesising
"Pulling this together, what I'm seeing is..."
"The data points to one root cause..."
"Based on everything we've covered..."

Body Language & Presence

✅ Positive Signals

  • Lean slightly forward — shows engagement
  • Maintain comfortable eye contact (70–80%)
  • Write on paper visibly while structuring
  • Nod when receiving data — shows you're processing
  • Pause 2–3 seconds before answering a new question
  • Speak at 75% of your normal speed under pressure
  • Smile when appropriate — consulting is a human business

❌ Negative Signals

  • Looking at the ceiling or away when thinking
  • Fidgeting with pen / tapping nervously
  • Speaking too fast when nervous — slur and mumble
  • Upward inflection at end of statements (sounds uncertain)
  • Saying "umm/like/basically" excessively
  • Not writing anything — shows no structure
  • Checking the interviewer's face for approval too often

Recovering from Mistakes

Everyone makes calculation errors in case interviews. What matters is how you recover:

✅ The right approach: "Wait — let me check that. I think I made an error. [Recalculate briefly.] Yes, the correct number is X. That changes my conclusion slightly: [update]. Thank you for bearing with me." Then move on confidently. Don't apologise repeatedly. The interviewer wants to see you catch and correct — not spiral.

Reading Charts & Data Exhibits

Written cases and some interviewer-led cases present data exhibits. Knowing how to quickly extract insight from charts is a testable skill.

The TLDR Method for Charts

T
Title: Read the title and axes first. What is being measured? What time period? What unit (%, ₹, units)?
L
Level: What is the absolute level of the key metric? Is it high or low vs. what you'd expect?
D
Direction: Is the trend up, down, or flat? Is it accelerating or decelerating? When did direction change?
R
Relativity: Compare to benchmarks — other companies, prior periods, targets. An absolute number is meaningless without context.

What Each Chart Type Usually Shows

Chart Type Best For What to Look For
Line chart Trends over time Direction, inflection points, seasonality, comparative lines
Bar chart Comparisons across categories Relative size, outliers, ranking changes
Waterfall chart Bridge from one value to another Largest contributors to change (positive and negative)
Scatter plot Relationship between two variables Correlation direction, outliers, clusters
Pie / Donut Composition / share Dominant segments, unusual splits
Indexed chart Growth relative to a base period Which series grew faster; when divergence occurred
💡 The "so what?" test: After reading any chart, immediately ask "so what does this imply for the client?" A chart insight without an implication is incomplete. "Revenue grew 20%" → so what? "Revenue grew 20% while industry grew 35% → our relative market share is declining."
■ Communication

Written Case Studies

Written cases test the same skills as verbal cases but require a polished, structured document. You have more time — use it for depth, not just breadth.

Written Case Format

Typically: 1–2 hours to read a 15–30 page case pack + write a 3–8 page analysis OR a short slide deck. Sometimes includes financial modelling in Excel.

The Written Case Structure

1
Executive Summary (½ page) — Written last, placed first. State the problem, your recommendation, and the top 3 reasons in 3–4 sentences. The entire case must pass the "executive summary only" test.
2
Problem Diagnosis (1–2 pages) — What is happening and why? Use data from the exhibits to support each claim. Quantify where possible. Identify root causes.
3
Options Analysis (1–2 pages) — Present 2–3 strategic options. For each: pros, cons, financial implications, risks. Don't present only your preferred option — show you considered alternatives.
4
Recommendation (1 page) — State clearly which option you recommend and why. Include a sensitivity analysis if time allows. Address the biggest counter-argument.
5
Implementation Plan (½–1 page) — Timeline, key milestones, resources required, risks and mitigations, KPIs to track success.
💡 Time allocation (2-hour case): 20 min reading the case → 15 min structuring your approach → 45 min writing → 20 min refining and proofreading → 20 min writing the executive summary last.

Written Case Quality Standards

✅ Excellent Written Case

  • Every claim supported by data from exhibits
  • Numbers clearly calculated and referenced
  • Headers and sub-headers create easy navigation
  • Executive summary can stand alone
  • Recommendation is specific and actionable
  • Risks acknowledged and mitigated
  • Professional language, no typos

❌ Weak Written Case

  • Assertions without data support
  • Analysis summary without a recommendation
  • One giant wall of text with no structure
  • Recommendation buried at the end
  • No consideration of risks or alternatives
  • Vague next steps ("the company should improve")
  • Exhibits referenced but not interpreted

# Mistake Why It Fails The Fix
1 Jumping to a solution before structuring Shows reactive, unstructured thinking Always pause 60–90 sec to build a framework first
2 Going too narrow too fast (one branch) Misses the real issue in the other branch Always breadth-first — full tree before diving deep

Interview Q&A — What Interviewers Really Want to Hear

Q: Walk me through how you would structure a case about a company's declining profits.
I'd start by clarifying the scope — is this a single product, division, or the whole company? And is the industry also experiencing this, or is it specific to our client? Then I'd structure the analysis as: Profit = Revenue − Costs. On the revenue side, I'd look at whether it's a price issue or a volume issue — volume breaks down into customer acquisition and retention, and price includes mix shift. On the cost side, I'd split into fixed costs (rent, salaries, depreciation) and variable costs (COGS, labour, logistics). I'd also benchmark the client against industry peers and historical performance to understand whether this is a structural or cyclical issue. I'd prioritise whichever branch shows the biggest gap, then drill to root cause and recommend targeted interventions.
Q: How do you estimate the number of petrol pumps in India?
I'll use a demand-side approach. India has ~300 million registered vehicles. Average vehicle is driven ~30 km/day × 300 days = 9,000 km/year. Fuel efficiency ~15 km/litre → ~600 litres/year per vehicle. Total fuel demand = 300M × 600 = 180 billion litres/year. A petrol pump serves ~2,000 litres/day × 300 operating days = 600,000 litres/year. Number of pumps = 180B ÷ 600K = 300,000 pumps. Sanity check: IOCL alone has ~30,000 pumps; 4 major OMCs → ~120,000 company-owned + dealer pumps. My estimate of 300,000 feels reasonable as a total including all dealer-operated stations.
Q: Our client should definitely enter this new market. I strongly disagree with your recommendation not to. What do you say?
I appreciate the challenge, and I want to make sure I'm not missing something important. Could you share what specific evidence or factor is leading you toward entry? [Listen carefully.] If the point raised is new information I hadn't weighted — I'd update: "That's a fair point — I hadn't fully weighted [factor]. Given that, I'd revise my view toward entry, specifically via [mode]." If it's the same information I'd already considered: "I understand the instinct, and entry is attractive on paper. However, my concern remains: [evidence A and evidence B]. The risk of undercapitalised entry into this incumbent-dominated market exceeds the expected return in the 5-year window. That said, if we could structure entry via a joint venture to limit downside, I'd be more comfortable recommending it."
Q: Tell me about a time you solved a complex analytical problem.
Use the STAR format — Situation, Task, Action, Result. The "Action" should demonstrate structured thinking and data use. Example: "In my college project, our team was tasked with [Situation]. I was responsible for [Task]. I structured the analysis by [breaking it into X and Y], gathered data from [sources], identified that [key insight], and recommended [specific action]. As a result, [quantified outcome]." The best STAR stories show you were the driver, faced real ambiguity, used data, and created measurable impact.

Quick-Fire Reference

Question Answer
What is MECE? Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive — no overlaps, no gaps in your analysis buckets
What is the Pyramid Principle? Lead with the conclusion/recommendation, follow with supporting arguments, then supporting evidence
Profit = ? Revenue − Costs. Revenue = Price × Volume. Costs = Fixed + Variable.
TAM vs SAM vs SOM? Total Addressable Market → Serviceable Addressable Market → Serviceable Obtainable Market (our realistic share)
When to use Porter's 5 Forces? Assessing industry attractiveness and competitive dynamics — especially for market entry, competitive response, or strategy cases
What is a STAR answer? Situation → Task → Action → Result. Used for behavioural / fit interview questions.
How many clarifying questions should you ask? 1–2 targeted, high-value questions. Not more — you should derive most context from the prompt itself.
What is synergy in M&A? Value created from combining two companies that wouldn't exist independently — revenue synergies (cross-sell) and cost synergies (shared overhead)
What does "boiling the ocean" mean? Analysing everything exhaustively without prioritising — the opposite of the 80/20 principle. A common mistake in case interviews.
How do you handle not knowing an industry? Say so briefly, apply general principles, and show you can reason from first principles: "I don't have deep experience here, so I'll treat this as [analogous industry] and adjust for [key differences]."
