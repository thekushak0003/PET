■ Foundations

What is a Project / Product Methodology?

A methodology is a structured set of principles, practices, roles, and processes that guide how a team plans, executes, and delivers a product or project. Choosing the right methodology is one of the most critical decisions in product development.

Why Methodologies Matter

Predictability

A shared process gives stakeholders confidence in timelines, scope, and delivery quality — reducing ambiguity and panic.

Team Alignment

Everyone knows who does what, when, and how decisions are made. Reduces duplicated work and misunderstandings.

Continuous Improvement

Methodologies embed feedback loops — retrospectives, reviews, inspections — so teams get better sprint over sprint.

Stability of requirements, team size, regulatory context, and risk tolerance all influence where on this spectrum you should operate.

How to Choose a Methodology

Factor Choose Predictive (Waterfall) Choose Adaptive (Agile/Scrum)
Requirements Stable, well-understood upfront Evolving, unclear, or customer-driven
Timeline Fixed, contractually defined Flexible — time-boxed iterations
Customer involvement Low (sign-off at key gates) High (continuous feedback)
Team Specialised silos, large teams Cross-functional, co-located
Regulatory High compliance, audit trails needed Lower regulation, internal products
Risk tolerance Low (no surprises) Higher (fail fast, learn fast)
Examples Construction, defence, pharma, ERP rollouts SaaS products, mobile apps, startups
💡 Real world: Most mature product teams use a hybrid — Waterfall-like planning at the programme level (roadmap, releases) and Agile/Scrum at the team level (sprint execution).

Iron Triangle — The Trade-off

Scope / Cost / Time

Every project is constrained by three variables. You can only fix two — the third must flex:

  • Waterfall fixes Scope + Time → Cost flexes (budget overruns common)
  • Agile fixes Time (sprint) + Cost (team size) → Scope flexes (backlog adjusted)
  • Kanban fixes neither strictly → continuous optimisation of flow and throughput

Agile vs Waterfall — The Classic Interview Comparison

When Waterfall Wins

  • Requirements are legally locked (medical devices, defence)
  • Team is distributed across time zones with poor communication
  • Fixed-price, fixed-scope contracts with legal penalties
  • Hardware manufacturing where changes are expensive
  • Regulatory compliance requires audit trails

When Agile Wins

  • Requirements evolve with user feedback (SaaS, apps)
  • Market is moving fast — need to ship and learn quickly
  • Team is co-located or has strong collaboration tools
  • Product vision is clear but implementation path is uncertain
  • Business value needs to be delivered incrementally
■ Agile · Core

The Agile Manifesto & Values

The Agile Manifesto was written in 2001 by 17 software practitioners. It is not a methodology — it is a mindset that all Agile frameworks (Scrum, XP, Kanban) are built upon.

The 4 Core Values

Agile Manifesto — 2001

"We are uncovering better ways of developing software by doing it and helping others do it. Through this work we have come to value…"

🧑‍🤝‍🧑
Individuals and interactions over processes and tools
Great processes can't fix a broken team. Human collaboration is the core engine of delivery.
🚀
Working software over comprehensive documentation
The primary measure of progress is a working product, not documents. "Just enough" documentation.
🤝
Customer collaboration over contract negotiation
Work with the customer throughout delivery — not just at the start to sign a contract and at the end to deliver.
🔄
Responding to change over following a plan
Plans become stale. The ability to adapt quickly to new information is a competitive advantage.

"That is, while there is value in the items on the right, we value the items on the left more."

⚠️ Common misconception: Agile does NOT mean "no planning" or "no documentation." It means planning frequently in smaller chunks and having just enough documentation to be useful — not comprehensive specs that no one reads.
■ Agile · Principles

The 12 Agile Principles

Behind the four values are 12 principles that translate the mindset into actionable guidance. These are tested heavily in PM interviews.

# Principle What It Means in Practice
1 Satisfy the customer through early and continuous delivery of valuable software Ship working features fast. Don't wait for "perfect." Release often.
2 Welcome changing requirements, even late in development Change = competitive advantage. Embrace it via backlog management.
3 Deliver working software frequently (weeks not months) Short sprints (1–4 weeks). Get feedback before investing more.
4 Business and developers must work together daily Product Owner is always available. No throwing specs over the wall.
5 Build around motivated individuals. Give them the environment and trust Servant leadership. No micromanagement. Remove blockers.
6 Face-to-face conversation is the most efficient communication Stand-ups, pairing, demos. Prefer conversation over long emails.
7 Working software is the primary measure of progress No partial credit for "90% done." Only shipped counts.
8 Agile processes promote sustainable development (constant pace) Avoid crunch. 40-hour weeks. Burnout kills quality and velocity.
9 Continuous attention to technical excellence and good design Technical debt compounds. Refactoring, testing, clean code matter.
10 Simplicity — the art of maximising work not done Don't build features nobody asked for. YAGNI (You Ain't Gonna Need It).
11 The best architectures, requirements, and designs emerge from self-organising teams Don't prescribe how — prescribe the outcome. Teams know their work best.
12 Regularly reflect and adjust to become more effective (retrospectives) Build a culture of learning. Every sprint ends with a retrospective.

Agile Mindset vs Agile Process

Agile Mindset (culture)

  • Trust the team to solve problems
  • Embrace uncertainty and experimentation
  • Optimise for learning, not just output
  • Customer outcomes over feature velocity
  • Transparency about blockers and failures

Agile Process (ceremonies)

  • Running daily stand-ups mechanically
  • Filling in story point spreadsheets
  • Holding retrospectives nobody acts on
  • Using a Kanban board as a to-do list
  • "Going through the motions" of Scrum
💡 "Dark Scrum" / "Cargo Cult Agile": Doing Agile ceremonies without the mindset — adding sprint ceremonies to an otherwise waterfall process. Sprints become mini-waterfalls with the same dysfunction.
■ Scrum · Roles & Artifacts

Scrum Roles & Artifacts

Scrum is the most widely used Agile framework. It is built on three pillars (Transparency, Inspection, Adaptation) and three commitments. Know every role, artifact, and event cold.

Three Pillars of Scrum (Empiricism)

Transparency

The process and work must be visible to those performing and inspecting it. Common language, shared Definition of Done, open backlogs.

Inspection

Frequent examination of progress and artifacts to detect undesirable variances. Done through Sprint Review, Daily Scrum, Retrospective.

Adaptation

If inspection reveals deviation, the process or product must be adjusted as soon as possible to minimise further deviation.

The Three Scrum Roles

🧑‍💼
Product Owner (PO)
One person (not a committee). Maximises product value. Owns and orders the Product Backlog. Only person who can accept or reject work. Represents the voice of the customer. Accountable for ROI.

Key skills: prioritisation, stakeholder management, clear communication, domain knowledge.
🧑‍🏫
Scrum Master (SM)
Servant leader. Not a project manager. Facilitates Scrum events, removes impediments (blockers), coaches team on Scrum, shields team from external distractions.

Serves the PO, the Developers, and the organisation. Accountable for Scrum adoption and effectiveness.
👩‍💻
Developers
Cross-functional team of 3–9 people. Self-organising — they decide how to do the work. Includes all skills needed: dev, QA, design, data. No sub-teams or titles. Collectively accountable for delivery.

Create the Sprint Backlog and own the Definition of Done.

The Three Scrum Artifacts

📦 Product Backlog

The ordered list of everything that might be needed in the product. Owned by the Product Owner. Never "complete" — evolves as product and market evolve. Items higher up are more refined (ready to work on); lower items are rough ideas. Refined continuously in Backlog Refinement sessions.

📋 Sprint Backlog

The set of Product Backlog items selected for the Sprint plus the plan for delivering them. Created during Sprint Planning. Owned by the Developers. Updated daily. Visible to all — anyone can see what's being worked on and what impediments exist.

✅ Increment

The sum of all completed Product Backlog items during a Sprint, plus all previous Sprints. Must meet the Definition of Done (DoD). Must be in a usable condition regardless of whether the PO decides to release it. The DoD is the shared, explicit quality standard.

Definition of Done (DoD) vs Acceptance Criteria

Definition of Done

Team-wide quality standard applied to every Product Backlog Item. Ensures transparency and shared understanding. Example: code reviewed, unit tests written, integration tested, documentation updated, deployed to staging.

Acceptance Criteria

Specific conditions for a particular user story to be accepted by the PO. Written by PO, agreed with Developers. Example: "User can reset password via email. Email arrives within 60 seconds."

■ Scrum · Events

Scrum Events & The Sprint Cycle

The Sprint

A Sprint is a fixed time-box of 1–4 weeks (typically 2 weeks) during which a "Done," usable, potentially releasable product Increment is created. Sprints have a consistent duration throughout the project. A new Sprint starts immediately after the conclusion of the previous Sprint.

💡 Sprint cancellation: Only the Product Owner can cancel a Sprint — and only if the Sprint Goal becomes obsolete (e.g., due to a market pivot). This is rare and disrupts the team significantly.

The Five Scrum Events

1. Sprint PlanningMax 8h (2-week sprint)
Whole Scrum Team. Define Sprint Goal. Select PBIs from Product Backlog. Developers plan how they'll deliver the work. Output: Sprint Goal + Sprint Backlog. Answer: "What can we deliver and how?"
2. Daily Scrum (Stand-up)Max 15 min, same time/place
Developers only (PO/SM may attend but don't run it). Three questions: What did I do yesterday? What will I do today? Any blockers? Inspects progress toward Sprint Goal. Synchronises work — NOT a status report to management.
3. Sprint ReviewMax 4h (2-week sprint)
Whole Scrum Team + stakeholders. Demo the Increment. Inspect outcomes vs Sprint Goal. Gather feedback. PO clarifies backlog based on new information. Informal, collaborative — NOT a sign-off ceremony.
4. Sprint RetrospectiveMax 3h (2-week sprint)
Scrum Team only. Inspect: people, relationships, process, tools. What went well? What didn't? What one improvement will we commit to next sprint? Output: actionable improvement items added to Sprint Backlog.
5. Backlog Refinement~10% of sprint capacity (informal)
Not an official Scrum event, but a recommended activity. PO + Developers. Break down large items (epics). Add detail, estimates, acceptance criteria. Ensures top of backlog is always "sprint ready."
Summary TimeboxPer sprint
Sprint Planning (8h) + Daily Scrums (15min × days) + Review (4h) + Retro (3h) = ~15–20h of meetings per 2-week sprint. Everything else is development time.

■ Scrum · Metrics

Estimation, Velocity & Scrum Metrics

Story Points & Estimation

Story points estimate the relative complexity and effort of a user story — not time. They account for effort, complexity, risk, and uncertainty together.

Technique How It Works Best For
Planning Poker Each team member secretly picks a Fibonacci card (1,2,3,5,8,13,21). Reveal simultaneously. Discuss outliers. Re-vote until consensus. Sprint planning sessions; surfacing hidden complexity
T-Shirt Sizing Assign XS/S/M/L/XL to stories. Quick, visual. Map to points later. Early estimation, epics, roadmap level
Bucket System Place stories in numbered buckets. Fast for large backlogs. Estimating 50+ items quickly
Affinity Mapping Sort stories by relative size without individual scoring. Group into bands. Large backlog refinement
#NoEstimates Count stories completed per sprint instead of estimating points. Assumes stories are broken to similar sizes. Mature teams with consistent story sizing
💡 Fibonacci sequence for estimation: 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21, 34. The growing gaps force teams to acknowledge uncertainty. Estimating "7 points" vs "8 points" is false precision — "5 or 8?" is the real question.

Key Scrum Metrics

📈
Velocity
Story points completed per sprint. Used for forecasting. Stabilises after 3–5 sprints. Never use as a performance target.
📉
Burndown Chart
Remaining work vs time. Ideal line shows expected pace. Team below = ahead. Above = at risk of not completing sprint.
📊
Burnup Chart
Work completed vs total scope. Handles scope changes better than burndown. Shows both progress AND scope changes clearly.
🎯
Sprint Goal Hit Rate
% of sprints where the Sprint Goal was met. Better measure of team health than velocity. Target: >80%.
🔄
Cycle Time
Time from work starting to work done. Shorter = faster feedback. Track per story type.
💡
Escaped Defects
Bugs found in production vs bugs found in sprint. Tracks quality. Ideally zero per sprint.

Common Scrum Anti-Patterns

Anti-Pattern Description Fix
Zombie Scrum Going through ceremonies with no real collaboration or improvement Leadership buy-in, psychological safety, genuine retrospectives
Sprint Zero A "setup sprint" before real sprints — often no increment produced Do just enough setup, include in Sprint 1, produce an increment
Hardening Sprint Extra sprint at end to fix bugs and technical debt Build quality in every sprint via strong DoD and TDD
PO as Proxy PO can't make decisions — goes up to management for approval Empower PO with real authority over backlog and budget
Velocity as KPI Management optimises for points — teams inflate estimates Use velocity for forecasting only, never as a target
Missing Retrospectives Team skips retros when "busy" — largest driver of Scrum failure Retros are non-negotiable. Shorter is better than skipped.
■ Kanban · Principles

Kanban Core Principles & The Board

Kanban (看板 = "visual signal" in Japanese) originated in Toyota's manufacturing system. It is a method for managing and improving knowledge work with an emphasis on flow and limiting work in progress.

The 4 Core Properties of Kanban

1. Visualise the Workflow

Make all work and its status visible on a board. Exposes hidden work, blockers, and bottlenecks that are invisible in email and spreadsheets.

2. Limit Work In Progress (WIP)

Set explicit limits on how many items can be in each column. Forces focus. Prevents multitasking. Surfaces bottlenecks. The core mechanic of Kanban.

3. Manage Flow

Optimise the smooth movement of work through the system. Measure cycle time. Identify and remove blockers. Flow efficiency > individual efficiency.

4. Make Policies Explicit

Define clear rules: definition of "done" per column, WIP limits, classes of service (expedite, standard, fixed date). Everyone knows the rules.

5. Implement Feedback Loops

Regular reviews: daily stand-up at the board, replenishment meetings, delivery reviews, retrospectives. At multiple cadences.

6. Improve Collaboratively, Evolve Experimentally

Use the scientific method. Small changes. Measure impact. Kanban is evolutionary — it doesn't prescribe a future state.

■ Kanban · Metrics

Kanban Metrics & Flow

The Four Key Kanban Metrics

Metric Definition Formula / Tool Why It Matters
Cycle Time Time from work starting to work done (item in progress to shipped) Done date − Start date Predictability. If cycle time is consistent, you can forecast delivery.
Lead Time Time from customer request to delivery (backlog entry to shipped) Done date − Request date Customer experience metric. Measures end-to-end responsiveness.
Throughput Number of items completed per time period Items/week or items/sprint Capacity and productivity. Use for forecasting how much can be done.
WIP (Work in Progress) Number of items started but not yet completed Count of active items Little's Law: Cycle Time = WIP / Throughput. Lower WIP → faster cycle time.

Little's Law

Little's Law
Cycle Time = WIP ÷ Throughput

The fundamental law of queuing theory — applies to any stable system

⟹ To reduce cycle time by 50%, either halve the WIP (limit to 5) OR double the throughput. Halving WIP is usually far easier.

Cumulative Flow Diagram (CFD)

The CFD shows the quantity of work in each state over time. A healthy CFD shows all bands growing steadily and in parallel. Warning signs:

  • Widening band — work piling up in that state (bottleneck)
  • Flat line — no work moving through (blocked stage)
  • Narrowing band — work draining from that stage faster than it's replenished (upstream bottleneck)
💡 The vertical distance between two band lines = WIP in that stage. The horizontal distance between entering and exiting a stage = cycle time through that stage.
■ Waterfall · Phases

Waterfall Phases & Lifecycle

Waterfall is a sequential, linear project management methodology where each phase must be completed and signed off before the next begins. Named for the way each phase "flows" into the next.

The Seven Phases

1
Requirements Gathering & Analysis
Stakeholders define all requirements upfront. What does the system need to do? Who are the users? What are the constraints? Produces a detailed, signed-off requirements document (BRD/SRS).
Output: Business Requirements Document (BRD), System Requirements Specification (SRS)
2
System Design
Architects design the technical solution: system architecture, database design, interface design, technology stack, integration points. High-Level Design (HLD) then Low-Level Design (LLD).
Output: Architecture Document, Database Schema, API Specs, UI Wireframes
3
Implementation (Coding)
Developers build the system according to the design specifications. Often the longest phase. Work is divided by modules or subsystems. Unit testing happens here.
Output: Source code, unit test reports, build artifacts
4
Integration & Testing
System Testing (SIT), User Acceptance Testing (UAT), Performance Testing, Security Testing. Bugs found here are expensive to fix — requirements may need revisiting.
Output: Test plans, bug reports, UAT sign-off, test summary report
5
Deployment
System is released to the production environment. Includes data migration, cutover planning, rollback procedures, and go-live support. Often a high-risk, high-coordination event.
Output: Deployment plan, release notes, go-live sign-off
6
Maintenance & Support
Ongoing bug fixes, patches, performance tuning, and minor enhancements. The longest-running phase. Any major new features restart the waterfall lifecycle.
Output: Patch releases, maintenance logs, change requests

Strengths & Weaknesses

Strengths

  • Clear structure and well-defined stages
  • Easy to understand and explain to stakeholders
  • Detailed documentation for compliance and auditing
  • Good for projects with stable, fixed requirements
  • Easier to coordinate across large, distributed teams
  • Clear milestones and sign-off gates

Weaknesses

  • No working software until very late in the project
  • Changes are very expensive — going back a phase is costly
  • Customer doesn't see the product until UAT
  • Risk of building the wrong thing (requirements decay)
  • Testing happens too late — bugs are expensive to fix
  • Poor fit for innovative or uncertain products
■ Waterfall · Documentation

Documentation, Phase Gates & SDLC

Phase Gate Reviews

Between each Waterfall phase, a Gate Review (also called a Stage Gate or Tollgate) is held. Stakeholders decide whether to:

Go

Phase outputs meet criteria. Proceed to next phase with full approval.

Conditional Go

Minor issues to resolve. Proceed with specific conditions and follow-up actions.

No Go (Hold / Kill)

Significant problems. Hold until resolved or kill the project entirely.

Key Documents in Waterfall

Document Phase Contents
Project Charter Initiation Business justification, objectives, scope boundaries, key stakeholders, budget authority
BRD (Business Requirements Doc) Requirements What the business needs — functional and non-functional requirements, use cases
SRS (System Requirements Spec) Requirements Technical translation of BRD — detailed system behaviour specifications
HLD (High-Level Design) Design Architecture, major components, technology choices, system interfaces
LLD (Low-Level Design) Design Detailed module design, class diagrams, database schemas, API contracts
Test Plan Testing Scope, approach, resources, schedule, pass/fail criteria for each test type
UAT Sign-off Testing Customer/sponsor formal acceptance that the system meets agreed requirements
Deployment Plan Deployment Cutover steps, rollback procedures, post-go-live monitoring plan

■ Lean Principles

Lean Product Development

Lean originated in Toyota's Production System (TPS). Applied to software by Mary and Tom Poppendieck, Lean focuses on eliminating waste and delivering maximum value with minimum effort.

The 7 Lean Principles (Poppendieck)

# Principle Application in Product Development
1 Eliminate Waste Remove anything that doesn't add customer value: unused features, waiting time, unnecessary approvals, redundant documentation, defects.
2 Amplify Learning Shorten feedback loops. Prototype early. A/B test. Write tests before code. Learn from failure fast and cheaply.
3 Decide as Late as Possible Delay irreversible decisions until you have more information. Keep options open. Don't overbuild upfront.
4 Deliver as Fast as Possible Speed = learning velocity. Continuous delivery. Small batches. CI/CD pipelines. The faster you ship, the faster you learn.
5 Empower the Team Those closest to the work make the decisions. Self-organising teams. Leaders remove obstacles, don't command.
6 Build Integrity In Quality built in from the start (TDD, code reviews, CI). Refactoring is not optional. Perceived and conceptual integrity.
7 See the Whole Optimise the system, not local parts. A fast dev team and slow QA = no improvement. Measure end-to-end flow.

The 7 Types of Waste (TIMWOOD)

T — Transport

Moving items unnecessarily. E.g., handoffs between too many teams.

I — Inventory

Work started but not delivered. Large backlog, unused features.

M — Motion

People moving unnecessarily. Context switching between projects.

W — Waiting

Waiting for approvals, reviews, environments, dependencies.

O — Overproduction

Building features nobody asked for. Gold plating.

O — Over-processing

More process, approval steps, or documentation than needed.

D — Defects

Bugs, rework, and technical debt. Found late = more expensive.

+ Skills

Underutilising team talent. Not involving developers in design.

■ SAFe & Scaled Agile

Scaling Agile — SAFe, LeSS & Spotify

When Agile needs to scale beyond one team, scaled frameworks provide coordination structures. Scrum works well for 1 team (3–9 people). Scaled frameworks handle 5–150+ teams.

Scaled Agile Framework (SAFe)

SAFe is the most widely adopted scaled Agile framework. It organises work across three levels:

1
Team Level
Individual Agile teams (Scrum or Kanban). 2-week sprints. Deliver working software every iteration.
2
Program Level — Agile Release Train (ART)
5–12 teams coordinated on a single Agile Release Train. Operate on a Program Increment (PI) of 8–12 weeks (typically 4 sprints + 1 innovation/planning sprint). All teams align on PI Objectives at the PI Planning event (the "big room planning").
3
Portfolio Level
Strategic themes, Lean portfolio management, budget allocation via Lean Budgets (not project-by-project). Epics prioritised at the portfolio level.

Other Scaling Frameworks

Framework Key Idea Scale Complexity
SAFe (Scaled Agile Framework) Prescriptive framework with defined roles, events at team/program/portfolio levels 5–150+ teams High — significant training required
LeSS (Large-Scale Scrum) Extend Scrum minimally — one Product Owner, one Product Backlog, multiple teams 2–8 teams Medium — stays closer to Scrum
Nexus Add a Nexus Integration Team above 3–9 Scrum teams to coordinate integration 3–9 teams Low — Scrum extension only
Spotify Model Squads (teams), Tribes (collections of squads), Chapters (skill groups), Guilds (communities of practice) Flexible Cultural model, not a framework
⚠️ Spotify Model warning: The "Spotify Model" is often cargo-culted. Spotify themselves admitted it never worked exactly as documented. It's a cultural aspiration, not a blueprint to copy. Focus on the principles (autonomy, alignment, trust) not the structure.
■ XP, DSDM & Scrumban

XP, DSDM, Scrumban & Other Frameworks

Extreme Programming (XP)

XP is an Agile framework focused on engineering practices and technical excellence. It pushes good development practices to the "extreme."

Core XP Practices

  • Test-Driven Development (TDD) — Write test before code. Red → Green → Refactor.
  • Pair Programming — Two developers, one keyboard. Reduces bugs, spreads knowledge.
  • Continuous Integration (CI) — Commit multiple times daily. Automated build + test.
  • Refactoring — Continuously improve code without changing behaviour.
  • Simple Design — Do the simplest thing that could possibly work. YAGNI.
  • Collective Code Ownership — Anyone can improve any code anytime.
  • Small Releases — Release working software frequently.
  • On-site Customer — Real customer works with the team daily.

XP Values

Communication

Most problems come from lack of communication between developers and customers.

Simplicity

Do only what's needed now. Don't build for imagined future requirements.

Feedback

Short feedback cycles at every level — tests, customer review, retrospectives.

Courage

Tell the truth about estimates. Refactor fearlessly. Say no to scope creep.

DSDM (Dynamic Systems Development Method)

DSDM is an Agile framework that fixes time, cost, and quality while allowing scope to flex — the opposite of Waterfall. Popular in the UK, especially in government projects.

  • MoSCoW prioritisation — Must Have, Should Have, Could Have, Won't Have
  • Timeboxing — All work done in fixed time-boxes; scope adjusts to fit
  • Strong emphasis on business involvement at all stages
  • Produces a Business Case and holds a Feasibility phase before development

Scrumban

A hybrid of Scrum and Kanban. Keeps Scrum's structure (roles, sprint planning) while adding Kanban's flow visualisation and WIP limits. Used by teams transitioning between the two or handling both planned work and interrupt-driven work.

PRINCE2 (PRojects IN Controlled Environments)

A process-based, structured project management method widely used in the UK and Europe. Based on seven principles, seven themes, and seven processes. More governance-heavy than Agile. Often used alongside Agile: PRINCE2 Agile is a formal hybrid.

■ PM Practice

Roadmaps & Product Backlogs

The Product Roadmap

A roadmap communicates the direction and intent of a product over time. It is a strategic communication tool — NOT a commitment or a Gantt chart.

Now–Next–Later

Three buckets of work ordered by priority. No dates. Flexible. Best for early-stage products where dates are premature.

Quarterly Roadmap

Goals by quarter. "In Q1 we'll focus on X; Q2 on Y." Communicates themes without locking scope. Popular in OKR-driven organisations.

Timeline Roadmap

Features on a timeline with dates. Common in enterprises. Dangerous if treated as a commitment — becomes a mini Waterfall.

⚠️ Roadmap anti-pattern: A roadmap that lists every feature with exact dates is a Waterfall delivery plan, not a roadmap. It creates false certainty and makes course-correction politically difficult.

Backlog Hierarchy

Level Name Size Description
Strategic Theme / Initiative Months–years High-level strategic direction. "Improve user onboarding." Drives roadmap.
Portfolio Epic Weeks–months Large body of work spanning multiple sprints. "Complete checkout redesign." Broken into features.
Program Feature / Capability 1–4 sprints "Add Apple Pay support." Represents a meaningful, deliverable chunk of the epic.
Team User Story 1 sprint (ideally <= 3 days) "As a user, I can pay with Apple Pay at checkout." INVEST criteria.
Task Task / Sub-task Hours Individual technical tasks developers pull from stories. E.g., "Implement Apple Pay SDK."
■ PM Practice

User Stories, Personas & Jobs-to-be-Done

User Stories

A user story is a short, simple description of a feature from the user's perspective, written in everyday language.

The Template
As a [type of user], I want to [some goal], so that [some reason / value]
"As a new user, I want to reset my password via email, so that I can regain access to my account without contacting support."

INVEST Criteria — What Makes a Good Story

Letter Criterion Meaning
I Independent Can be developed and delivered without depending on other stories
N Negotiable Not a fixed contract — details evolve through conversation
V Valuable Delivers real value to the user or business — not just technical work
E Estimable Team can estimate the size (if not, it needs more detail or breaking down)
S Small Small enough to complete in one sprint (ideally 1–3 days)
T Testable Has clear acceptance criteria that can be verified

User Personas

A persona is a fictional character that represents a key user segment. Based on research, not assumptions. Used to keep the team focused on real user needs.

Persona Template
👩
Priya Sharma
UX Designer, 28
Background: Works at a mid-size agency. 5 years experience. Uses 4–6 tools daily. Values efficiency.
Goals: Deliver high-quality designs faster. Collaborate seamlessly with developers. Stay on top of feedback.
Pain Points: Too many context switches. Feedback scattered across Slack, email, and docs. Version confusion.
Quote: "I spend more time managing feedback than actually designing."

Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD)

JTBD (Clayton Christensen) focuses on the job a customer is "hiring" a product to do — the progress they want to make in their life — rather than their demographic profile.

JTBD Statement
When I [situation], I want to [motivation / job], so I can [expected outcome]
"When I'm about to present to a new client, I want to quickly find our best past work, so I can build credibility and win the project."
■ PM Practice

OKRs, Prioritisation & Decision Frameworks

OKRs (Objectives and Key Results)

OKRs is a goal-setting framework used by Google, Intel, Netflix, and most modern tech companies. It connects ambitious goals to measurable outcomes.

OKR Structure
Objective: What we want to achieve (qualitative, inspiring, ambitious)
"Become the most loved onboarding experience in our category"
Key Results: How we'll know we got there (quantitative, measurable, 3–5 per objective)
KR1: Increase activation rate from 42% → 70% by end of quarter
KR2: Reduce time-to-first-value from 14 days → 4 days
KR3: Achieve NPS of 50+ among users in first 30 days
💡 OKR rules: Objectives are inspiring but not measurable. Key Results are measurable but not tasks (they're outcomes, not outputs). If you hit 70% of your OKRs, that's considered a success — if you hit 100%, you weren't ambitious enough.

Prioritisation Frameworks

Framework Formula / Method Best For Limitation
RICE (Reach × Impact × Confidence) ÷ Effort. Numerical score per feature. Data-informed prioritisation, removing bias Reach and Impact are often estimated, not measured
ICE Score Impact × Confidence × Ease. Simpler version of RICE. Quick scoring for growth experiments Very subjective; no reach component
MoSCoW Must Have / Should Have / Could Have / Won't Have Scope management, sprint planning Subjective; "Must Have" category inflates quickly
Kano Model Classifies: Basic (expected), Performance (linear), Delight (unexpected joy) Understanding which features delight vs satisfy Requires customer surveys to classify correctly
Value vs Effort Matrix 2×2: High value / Low effort = Quick Wins (do first) Visual, quick team alignment sessions Oversimplified; doesn't capture risk or dependencies
Weighted Scoring Score criteria (strategic fit, revenue, NPS impact) × weights = total Aligning features to strategic goals Weights are subjective; garbage in, garbage out

The RICE Scoring Model

RICE Formula
RICE Score = (Reach × Impact × Confidence%) ÷ Effort
Reach
How many users affected per time period? (e.g., 500 users/quarter)
Impact
3=massive, 2=high, 1=medium, 0.5=low, 0.25=minimal
Confidence
100%=high, 80%=medium, 50%=low. How sure are you?
Effort
Person-months of work for all people involved

Product Metrics & KPIs

The AARRR Pirate Metrics Framework

Stage Question Key Metrics
A — Acquisition How do users find you? Traffic, CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost), CPC, CTR, sessions by source
A — Activation Do users have a great first experience? Signup rate, time-to-first-value, onboarding completion rate, activation rate
R — Retention Do users come back? DAU/MAU ratio, churn rate, retention cohorts (D1/D7/D30), session frequency
R — Referral Do users tell others? NPS (Net Promoter Score), viral coefficient (K-factor), referral rate
R — Revenue Do you make money? MRR/ARR, ARPU, LTV, LTV:CAC ratio, conversion rate, expansion revenue

Key Product Metrics Glossary

Metric Formula What It Tells You
DAU / MAU Ratio Daily Active Users ÷ Monthly Active Users Stickiness. 50%+ = highly sticky (WhatsApp). <10% = low engagement.
Churn Rate Customers lost / Customers at start of period How fast you're losing customers. The silent killer of SaaS.
Customer LTV ARPU × Average Customer Lifetime (= 1/Churn) Revenue per customer over their entire relationship with you.
CAC Total Sales + Marketing Spend ÷ New Customers Cost to acquire one customer. Must be < LTV to be sustainable.
LTV:CAC Ratio LTV ÷ CAC 3:1 is healthy. <1:1 = losing money on every customer.
NPS % Promoters − % Detractors (from 0–10 scale) -100 to +100. Above 50 = excellent. Below 0 = serious problems.
MRR Monthly Recurring Revenue = active subscribers × avg plan price Predictable revenue base. Foundation of SaaS valuation.
Activation Rate Users who completed key action ÷ total signups Quality of onboarding. Low activation = friction before value.
Time-to-Value (TTV) Time from signup to first meaningful value moment Shorter = better. The "aha moment" must come fast.

North Star Metric

A single metric that best captures the core value your product delivers to customers. Drives alignment across all teams. Examples:

Spotify

Time spent listening per user per month — measures music value delivered.

Airbnb

Nights booked — captures both host and guest value in one number.

Slack

Messages sent per organisation — measures actual team communication activity.

Facebook

Daily Active Users — measures habitual engagement with the platform.

WhatsApp

Messages sent per day — simple proxy for core communication value.

Medium

Total reading time — aligns writers (who want readers) and readers (who want content).

■ Interview Prep

Top PM Methodology Interview Questions

The most frequently asked product management methodology questions across tech and consulting placements.

Q1What is the difference between Agile and Scrum?
Agile is a mindset and philosophy — a set of values and principles for software development (codified in the 2001 Agile Manifesto). Scrum is a specific framework that implements Agile principles. Think of Agile as the "what" (deliver value iteratively, embrace change) and Scrum as the "how" (defined roles like PO and Scrum Master, prescribed events like Sprint Planning and Retrospectives, fixed sprint lengths). You can be Agile without doing Scrum, but Scrum is always Agile.
Q2When would you choose Waterfall over Agile?
I'd choose Waterfall when: (1) Requirements are fixed and well-understood upfront with little chance of change — e.g., regulatory compliance systems. (2) The project has a fixed-price, fixed-scope contract with legal penalties for scope changes. (3) Heavy documentation and audit trails are required — medical devices, defence, government. (4) The team is large and distributed with specialised silos. (5) Hardware is involved where changes are physically expensive. For anything innovative, user-facing, or uncertain, I'd default to Agile.
Q3What is the role of a Product Owner in Scrum?
The Product Owner is responsible for maximising the value of the product and the work of the Development Team. Key responsibilities: (1) Creating and clearly expressing Product Backlog items, (2) Ordering/prioritising the Product Backlog to best achieve goals, (3) Ensuring the backlog is visible, transparent, and understood, (4) Ensuring the team understands items to the level needed, (5) Only person who can cancel a sprint, accept sprint work, or reprioritise the backlog. The PO is one person, not a committee. They must be empowered with real decision-making authority — a PO who needs management approval for every decision cannot effectively fulfil the role.
Q4What are the three pillars of Scrum?
Transparency — the process and work must be visible to those performing and receiving the work (open backlog, shared DoD, visible impediments). Inspection — frequent examination of progress and artifacts to detect variances (Daily Scrum, Sprint Review, Retrospective). Adaptation — when inspection reveals deviation, the process or product must be adjusted immediately. These three pillars implement the empirical process of Scrum: learn by doing, not by predicting.
Q5What is the difference between a burndown and burnup chart?
A burndown chart shows remaining work (story points or tasks) vs time. It trends downward toward zero. It shows if you're on track to finish the sprint. Limitation: doesn't show scope changes — if you add 10 new stories mid-sprint, the burndown just goes back up, making it hard to tell what happened. A burnup chart shows completed work vs total scope — two lines: one going up (completed), one potentially moving (total scope). It clearly visualises both progress AND scope changes simultaneously. Burnup is more honest about scope changes.
Q6What is WIP limit in Kanban and why does it matter?
WIP (Work In Progress) limits set the maximum number of items allowed in a particular workflow stage at any one time. They matter because: (1) They expose bottlenecks — if a column is always at its WIP limit, work is piling up there (bottleneck revealed). (2) They reduce multitasking — forcing people to finish before starting new work. (3) They improve cycle time via Little's Law: Cycle Time = WIP ÷ Throughput. Lower WIP → faster delivery. (4) They create a pull system — new work is only pulled when there's capacity. The hardest part of Kanban is not creating the board — it's enforcing the WIP limits.
Q7How would you prioritise a backlog with 50 items and 2 weeks to deliver?
Step 1: Clarify the goal — what is the Sprint Goal or business objective for these 2 weeks? Step 2: Apply MoSCoW — classify each item as Must Have (non-negotiable), Should Have (important), Could Have (nice to have), Won't Have (out of scope). Step 3: Use RICE or value vs effort to score Must Haves against each other. Step 4: Check dependencies — some items may need others completed first. Step 5: Validate with team — get capacity estimates and confirm the sprint is achievable. Step 6: Set a Sprint Goal — a coherent theme that ties the selected items together. Never try to fit all 50 items — start with the 8–10 that deliver the most value toward the goal.
Q8What is the difference between Velocity and Throughput?
Velocity is a Scrum metric: the number of story points completed per sprint. It measures relative team capacity using an abstract unit (points). Because points are team-specific, you cannot compare velocity across teams. Throughput is a Kanban metric: the number of items completed per time period. It uses count of items — a concrete, objective measure. If teams size stories consistently, throughput is more honest and comparable than velocity. Both are forecasting tools, not performance targets. Using either as a management KPI causes gaming (inflating estimates, splitting stories artificially).

Question Answer
Who can cancel a sprint? Only the Product Owner — and only if the Sprint Goal becomes obsolete
Who prioritises the Product Backlog? The Product Owner — solely responsible for backlog ordering

What is a Definition of Done? Shared quality standard — criteria ALL increments must meet to be "done"
